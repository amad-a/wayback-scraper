WATER SOURCES

Introduction: Jerusalem Water Undertaking has to purchase more than 60% of its current drinking water supply from the Israeli Authorities. Even with these quantities, there was a real water shortage in the JWU distribution area during the last years which caused JWU to ration water supplies to the different areas. JWU sees a diehard reason for increasing the water sources in the area to accommodate the increasing demand for water especially since there will be an expected population increase because of the peace effort in the area. Hydrologists and Hydrogeological engineers pointed out certain sites in which drilling for water should commence. One of these sites is the Ein Samia well field which is located in the Eastern Groundwater Basin. After drilling Well No. 6 in this area, it is believed that any further drilling in this vicinity will not be productive.

Ein Samia Well Field (Ein Samia Plains)

The Western Groundwater Basin of the West Bank is considered as one unit called the Nahr El-Auja-Tamaseeh Basin. Water is discharged seaward beneath the coastal plain. The annual renewable recharge of this basin is between 335-380 MCM. A small portion of the rainwater that seeps through the exposed parts of the Cenomanian and Turonian aquifers in the Western Groundwater Basin flows in springs draining the sloping aquifers - but most of the water reaches the regional aquifer and it is tapped by wells on both sides of the 1948 Armistice Line between the Palestinian Territory's West Bank and Israel - with the greater usage being now on the Israeli side.

From Israel's own reporting, it is stated that the high usage by the Israelis of the Western Groundwater Basin is causing the water level in this Western Basin to drop 0.79-1.03 meters/year. To keep the majority of the water of this aquifer for themselves, the Israeli government, who of course also controls the lands in the West Bank through its military forces and military laws, prevents Palestinian farmers (especially in the western half of the West Bank) from drilling new wells for agriculture and restrict rehabilitation and maintenance of their old wells.

Although most of the groundwater of the West Bank's Western Basin area is actually under the western part of the West Bank, these water resources are used in the water supply of Israel as mentioned above. The amount of water provided by this western basin of the West Bank supplies about 25% of Israel's total water supply through either natural discharge or by wells pumping within Israel. The overuse and misuse of this water basin by the Israelis is one of the most sensitive issues between Palestinians and Israelis.

The service area of the Jerusalem Water Undertaking (JWU) is partially located on this Palestinian Western Groundwater Basin. Despite the need for water in the area, the Israeli government did not allow during the 28 years of occupation any water resource development for JWU in this western part of the watershed.

Prior to the 1967 Israeli occupation of the West Bank, the Jordanian government did however drill five exploratory wells in the Western Basin in 1964 in order to test and monitor the availability of water in the area for the JWU. In fact, a pumping test was done on exploratory well No. 2 in the Shibteen area on May 6, 1967, only one month before the occupation. Since 1967, three of these exploratory wells have been capped and two are being pumped by the Israelis and these two wells serve about 22 villages and 6 Israeli settlements under the jurisdiction of the West Bank Water Department of the Israeli military government's civil administration and do not contribute in any way to the water distribution network under the jurisdiction of JWU.

JWU was supposed to utilize the Shibteen well field for the future demand of the central part of Palestine since it is obvious that the only water source left for fulfilling the demand is the Western Basin.

During the peace talks, the Palestinian negotiators insisted on their surface and groundwater rights in the West Bank, especially in the Western Basin.
