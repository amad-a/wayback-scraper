Feasts & Holidays

Muslim Feasts

Muslim holidays are dated according to a lunar calendar. They fall on different days of the Gregorian calendar in each year. The following are the most important Muslim and Christian holidays:

Eid al-Adha (Feast of the Sacrifice) commemorates the willingness and obedience of the Prophet Abraham to sacrifice his son Ishmael to God- and God's mercy in sending the Angel Gabriel to substitute a lamb for Ishmael. For Muslims on the Hajj this is the day when they make a sacrifice of an animal. After Eid al-Adha prayers Moslems normally sacrifice a ram, sheep, goat or cow - then divide the meat into three parts: one-third is given to the needy, one-third is distributed among family and friends, and one third is kept by the family. This four-day holiday is celebrated with good food, gifts to children, visits, and general merriment.

Ramadan, (The Holy Month of Fasting) the ninth month of the Muslim lunar calendar, marks the day when the Holy Qur'an was revealed to Prophet Muhammad through Angel Gabriel. Muslims spend the month of Ramadan fasting, with total abstention in order to experience the need of those who must go without. Everyday from sunrise to sunset, Muslims are prohibited from eating, drinking, and smoking. At sunset, families gather to break the fast with a meal called Iftar. Following Iftar, it is customary for Muslims to visit relatives, neighbors and friends. Celebrations often last all night with traditional sweets and drinks. The first threads of daylight signal the resumption of fasting. Ramadan is also a time when profanity, war, violence, and disagreement are prohibited, and a pervasive sense of community is fostered.

The Night of Decree (Laylat al-Qadar): the evening of the 27th day of Ramadan marks the most special day of the year for Muslims when Laylat al-Qadar is observed. Muslims believe that on this night in Jerusalem, Prophet Muhammad first received the revelation of the Holy Qur'an.

 

Eid il-Fitr: the first day of the next month after Ramadan, known as the month of Shawwal, marks the beginning of a three-day holiday called Eid il-Fitr when gifts are exchanged and family and friends are visited.

 

Christians Feasts

Christmas: The year's highlight in Bethlehem is Christmas. There are actually three celebrations: the Western observance occurs on December 25, according to the Gregorian calendar; the Orthodox Christmas is celebrated on January 6, according to the older Julian calendar; and finally the Armenian on January 19. On these days, the town is festive with lights and banners, and the churches are full of worshippers and visitors. Carols are chanted from loudspeakers in the crowded Manger Square, and brisk trade is done in the carved olive-wood and mother-of-pearl souvenirs for which Bethlehem is renowned.

Among the exciting aspects of Bethlehem's Christmas are the parades of Palestinian scouts through the town of Bethlehem. They mass in Manger Square to greet each Patriarch on his arrival from Jerusalem. Some scout processions start from Rachel's tomb and march to Manger Square. Others gather at Star Square for a shorter journey to Manger Square. Troops from various sectarian groupings march together in front of the Patriarchs' processions through the winding streets of town, with local people and foreign pilgrims, crowded together. When each Patriarch meets the dignitaries and then enters the church, the scouts disperse. The climax on Christmas eve for the Roman Catholics is the elaborate midnight Mass in the presence of dignitaries and diplomats. Other churches have their own special services in Bethlehem and Jerusalem. On Christmas day many people go to the Shepherds' Field Church in Beit Sahour to attend afternoon prayers in the old subterranean grotto.

Easter: During the Holy Week of Easter, pilgrims go to the Church of the Holy Sepulchre for redemption and acceptance by God. The Easter festival formally begins with Palm Sunday, when pilgrims carry palm branches and walk from Bethphage and the Mount of Olives down to the Church of the Holy Sepulchre. The calendar of events during the Holy Week offers a great variety of commemoration ceremonies. On Holy Thursday, the day of the ceremonial washing of feet, the Greek Orthodox Patriarch of Jerusalem performs the ceremony in the open area outside the Church of the Holy Sepulchre.
