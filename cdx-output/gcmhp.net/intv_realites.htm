“My Fight for Freedom”
An interview with Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj in
“Reality”, an Independent Tunisian weekly magazine

Seeing him, well built, bright, and mature, you might think of him as a movie star.  He has a presence that is so intense and so human.  Although he is not a playboy, he is seductive and overtaking by his fascinating engagement as a militant Palestinian for 30 years, first against Israeli Occupation, against all forms of violence and abuse of the law, and today against violence and abusing the law violence his compatriots.

He is also a psychiatrist in charge of a the Gaza Community Mental Health Program that offers mental health services to the population of the Gaza Strip including children of the Intifada, victims of a double loss: Lack of reference, destruction of their homes, and the death of their fathers. He has started a diploma project in community mental health in cooperation with seven international universities, one of which in Tunis University. He is also the Commissioner General of the Palestinian Independent Commission for Citizens’ Rights.

He is in Tunisia to mobilize support for his project. In this interview, we take the opportunity to talk with an authentic intellectual and a doctor who can be taken for the Franz Fanon of Palestinians.

Q: You are the Commissioner General of the Palestinian Independent Commission for Citizens’ Right.  What made you accept this position?

After the establishment of the Palestinian Authority, a decree was signed by President Arafat, in his authority as the president of Palestine, which founded this commission. It is an independent commission whose member is not appointed by the PA.

Q: What are the prerogatives of this commission?

We receive and direct all complaints of the citizens’ human rights violations, particularly in cases of violence are abuse of public authority that originates from State Agencies and there administration, from its security apparatus.

Q: For Example?

For example, last year we registered 550 complains from ordinary Palestinian citizens.  A large part of the complaints were of violations of human rights, bad treatment, illegal imprisonment, denying the rights etc…by the police force.  Also, there are complains against civil administration concerning cases of injustice. We also contributed to the reform of the Palestinian jurisdiction, and the totality of different laws than came from diverse origins (Israeli, Jordanian, Ottoman, and British). We are engaged in the process of Law reform and we have co-produced a text of basic laws.  Unfortunately, this text, which was received the ratified by the Legislative Council, was submitted to President Arafat, who has not signed it as of yet.
 
Q: The Palestinian Authority arrested you on several occasions. What are the reasons?

To start with, the commission was established by Hanan Ashraoui, the first Commissioner General. In June 1995, I was elected as Commissioner General, so I revised all the complaints submitted.  They were regarding violations of human rights by the Police and neglect by the Attorney General.  I addressed the Palestinian Authority pointing that the situation was serious and alarming.  I did not get any answer. I was arrested the following day and detained for 24 hours.  They told me not to engage myself in such affairs but I refused.
Few months later, there were signs of cooperation by the Authority on building its power through the Law.  We have thought that the page was turned, and a new era began.
However, after having denunciating all forms of oppression, I was arrested for 9 days.  I was well treated. Then Palestinian Authority received me and we had an understanding of a more convenient functioning.

Q: What is the security dimension of the Palestinian Power?  Is it the result of the American and Israeli pressure or is it in the nature of an authoritarian regime?

There is nothing than can justify the violation of law and human rights and victimizing citizens. In my opinion, nothing can justify violence, not even the excuse that Israel exercises pressure, which is by the way true.  America also exercises pressure.  It is true; however, these two countries are colonial, racist, and function with the logic of occupation.
With regards to the Arab culture of domination, the Palestinian Authority is formed from outside according to a system of a secretive and clandestine organization without a democratic tradition. We must develop consequently a democratic culture and respect of the power of law.

Q: How has Mohiydeen El-Sharief’s death affected the situation?

Up till that incident, there were improvements. We visited the prisoners and detained political activists. We had an agreement to visit prisoners after three days of detention, which is by the way not done in other Arab countries. There was also a signed charter concerning torture specially death under torture. Unfortunately, with the Mohiydeen El-Sharief affair, the situation regressed and became more complied. The Palestinian Authority accuses Hamas, who denies the accusations.  The result was arrests of Hamas activists.

Q: In relation to establishing the democratic process and Palestinians’ resistance under ten’s of years to the oppression of occupation, did the stuggle contribute to the establishment of a conscience and a real wish for democracy?  Is this the beginning of a civil Palestinian Society?

Certainly, there is a strong relationship between the Palestinian struggle for its liberty and the construction of a civil society.  We always demanded our right to democracy and resisted the violence exercised by the Israels.  Yet, we Palestinian rights were sometimes defended by Israelis. In some instances, the best lawyers for our cause were Israelis protesting against the military Israeli occupation. Incidentally, we were also the witnesses of a democratic political life in Israel, if for Jews only.  Israel is a democracy if you are a Jew. The Palestinian living in Israel and those of Gaza and the West Bank were witnesses to democratic institutions, laws, etc…
The Palestinians in Exile are different since they lived in a different political culture. That is why, in Palestine today there are two kinds of political cultures.  But there is a strong determination to construct a democratic society, and this can be felt in some PA figures as well as intellectuals.

Q: Are the children of the Intifada had dreams of freedom. You know it from being a psychiatric who worked with them.

During the Intifada, the Israeli occupation destroyed the two symbols of security: the fathers imprisoned, tortured, killed and the houses were demolished.  As a result, the children were traumatized when witnessing the murder of their fathers and the destruction of their houses.  The generation of the Intifada was profoundly disturbed.  Our research on the mental health in Gaza indicates that 15% of the population in Gaza were tortured and 20% were witnesses of grave violence including murder under Israeli occupation, 17% of the population suffer from psychiatric disorders depression and other psychiatric pathologies as a result of traumatism. Trauma is a serious product of violence.  Also the persons who were victims of violence are today aggressive to their families and the fellow citizens. We see this in households, in the streets, and in the prisons, where the guardians abuse their position of the authority. We understand the needs for rehabilitation to rights and justice. This conscience and determination is felt all over.

Q: During the Intifada you were a psychiatrist.  What is your role today?

I have established the Gaza Mental Health Program today.  We have six centers and we have exchanges of seven international universities. We have received help of Tunisian psychology’s trainees and cooperate with Prof. of Psychology A. Mahjoub from Tunis University. We have started a post graduate diploma in community mental health. We wish to receive Tunisian doctors and psychologists to help us in this program.  I have approached Tunisian Educators from the Ministry of Education in this respect.  In addition, we have projects for traumatized children, victims of torture, and women victims of violence.

Q: Do you think that this democratic process in Palestine could serve as a model and initiate change in the rest of the Arab World?

The Palestinian experience is unique in many facets.  We are still in the beginning of processes of building a civil society and a democratic state.
