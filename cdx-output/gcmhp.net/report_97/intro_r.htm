1996-1997 Report / Introduction
 
Within the Prison Walls

In February 1996, Israel slammed shut the prison doors, locking one million Palestinians within the teeming strip of land behind the gates of Gaza. Unemployment climbed to nearly 70% as tens of thousands were denied access to their jobs in Israel. With no means of subsistence, people struggled day and night to put food on the table. With a violent stranglehold, Israel devastated the economic, social, educational, family, religious, and medical links with the West Bank, Israel, and the outside world that help sustain life in Gaza. Tension mounted, violence rose, and social pressure escalated, tearing at the community and suffocating life. As the desperate conditions became an entrenched reality, the people of Gaza expressed their absolute frustration: “There’s no work, no money, no food.” The period of transition in Palestine had ended; the era of depression began.

From the day it imposed this strict closure on Palestine, Israel has spent the last two years perpetrating a seemingly endless string of hostilities, brutalizing the Palestinian people, vanquishing faith in the Oslo process, besieging Gaza, and further depressing the agony of Palestinian life. In May 1996, Israelis elected reactionary Benjamin Netanyahu to lead a new right-wing government. Over the following year and a half, that government has manipulated the fears and aspirations of the Palestinian Authority and the Palestinian people, shattering any remaining hope for peace. In September, Israel opened a tunnel under the Dome of the Rock in Jerusalem, provoking clashes during which Israeli soldiers killed 84 Palestinians and trampled the dreams of thousands. Laughing in the face of international condemnation, Israel began to build an illegal settlement on Jabal Abu Ghneim between Jerusalem and Bethlehem in March 1997. It seemed that there was nothing anyone could do. Hopelessness and despair set in. And the Israeli war machine has continued its siege: land confiscation has skyrocketed, settler violence has reached an all time high, home demolition is on the rise, thousands of Palestinians have been forced out of East Jerusalem, constant clashes have brought more and more death to Palestine, promised redeployments have not occurred, settlement building continues, and the closure remains. Although the official death of Oslo has not yet been announced, in their hearts, in their minds, and all around them the Palestinian people suffer the pain of broken dreams of peace and freedom.

Surrounded and squeezed from without, Palestinians have also faced increasing pressure from the social, economic, and political conditions within the prison walls. As closure strangles the economy, unemployment and prices spiral upward, confining most Palestinians to a life of extreme poverty and destitution. Stress and tension are abnormally high; many men feel choked because confined, inadequate because they cannot provide for their families. In stark contrast, the Palestinian Authority (PA) has abused the resources it controls, provoking widespread charges of corruption.

In addition, the PA seems unable to prevent or respond to the constantly escalating series of Israeli hostilities or even to resist pressure from Israel and the United States to abuse the rights of the Palestinian people. Thousands have been imprisoned, many tortured, and some even killed. Human rights activists, reporters, and outspoken critics have faced imprisonment and harassment for raising concern about PA policies. From December 1995 to June 1996, Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj, General Director of the GCMHP was arrested three times for standing up in defense of human rights for Palestinian in the course of his duty as Commissioner of the Palestinian Independent Commission for Citizens’ Rights.

Surviving in such deplorable political and economic conditions has torn at the fabric of the Palestinian community and the mental health of the Palestinian people. Anger, frustration, annoyance, inadequacy, fear, despondency, despair, failure, weakness, and hopelessness plague the minds of the Palestinian people. This tremendous emotional suffering remains bottled up because all avenues of release have been systematically shut down. Such rage cannot be suppressed forever and has begun to find an outlet in increased violence in Gaza. Hundreds of incidents of violence between clans have been reported over the last two years. Domestic violence has also reached unprecedented levels, reinforcing the structures of patriarchy and battering Palestinian women and children. Drug use, suicide, child labor, and divorce are all on the rise. Many people, especially the youth, are searching for possible outlets to escape the rising pressure, the devastated social, economic, political, and psychological conditions in which they are trapped. Indeed, Gaza has become a prison. As the doors remain tightly sealed and pressure builds from within, widespread depression has overtaken a great many people.

In this era of depression, the Gaza Community Mental Health Programme strives to meet the changing mental health needs of the Gaza community. Our special programs for women, children, victims of human rights abuse, and drug addicts focus on the sectors of Palestinian society that suffer the most violence, that face the most trauma. Prevention is playing an increasingly important role in the work of the GCMHP, as the various committees and projects reach out to the community through media events, video production, public meetings, and public awareness campaigns. Demonstrating that mental illness in the Palestinian context is a normal reaction to abnormal conditions, the GCMHP hopes to overcome the stigma attached to mental illness in order to encourage Palestinians to more freely and openly deal with their problems.

Facilitating the empowerment of women through rehabilitation, education, and training has always played an essential role in the work of the GCMHP. Over the last two years, our ‘training the trainers’ approach, designed to increase the number and distribution of such services, has generated impressive results. Training programs in Rafah and the Beach Camp have helped dozens of women gain the knowledge and skills which have allowed them to open new branches of the Women’s Empowerment Project in their communities. These centers now provide rehabilitation, education, and training to women throughout the Gaza Strip.

The core work of the GCMHP remains addressing the immediate mental health needs of a traumatized community. Treating almost 1200 new patients annually, the GCMHP clinical staff addresses the massive psychological afflictions of the Palestinian people. Despite the increasingly difficult context in which we work, our medical teams have achieved impressive success over the last two years.

One reason for the success of our clinical staff lies in the invaluable experience and training that they have received. The GCMHP continuously provides workshops, seminars, study days, courses, and many other training opportunities in order to increase the expertise of our staff. In 1997, our training activities took a giant step forward with the establishment of the first two-year Post Graduate Diploma in Community Mental Health to provide advtraining in the theory and practice of community mental health.

Under the current conditions of economic, political, and social devastation, the mental health of the Palestinian community is under siege. The mission of the GCMHP remains the same as ever: to address the tremendous psychological suffering of the Palestinian people and to enable the Gaza community to sustain the treatment and prevention of mental disorders long into the future. Our success over the last two years encourages us to continue. And the current situation demands that we reaffirm the commitment to help our people overcome the psychological effects of violence and confront the widespread despair and depression in which they are living. Our commitment remains unconditional, our determination remains steadfast. We will do all that we can to facilitate and support a healthy Palestinian community.
