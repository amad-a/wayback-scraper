Gaza Video Center
 
With visual demonstrations and clear explanations, documentary films can have a powerful effect in raising community awareness and in providing training services. Although video production is not new in Gaza, for many years none of the existing units produced documentaries or professional quality films from start to finish. Recognizing the need for such a unit, the GCMHP established the Gaza Video Center in late 1995. During 1996 and 1997, the Center’s video team received training and support and produced documentary films about psycho-social problems in Gaza.
 
While some of the Center’s crew had previous experience with local producers in Gaza, none had the expert training necessary to produce high-quality documentaries about mental health issues. From the C’s establishment, the team has received professional training from both local and international experts. In April 1996, two specialists from the Video Center’s Danish partner, “Final Cut Productions,” conducted a one-month training course for the staff on how to make documentaries. International guests of the GCMHP with knowledge of video production have also provided training for the team. Currently, the Center is preparing to conduct for its staff an advanced training course in video production. In addition, for the first time, the Video Center, in conjunction with the Women’s Empowerment Project, has begun training women in video and production. In the future, the Center hopes to offer more training for community members.
During the last two years, the Gaza Video Center has produced ten documentary films on Palestinian society. As the victims of many social problems in Gaza, children have been the focus of the majority of the Center’s films. Near to Death, concentrates on children living near Israeli settlements. Little Hands discusses the rising phenomena of child labor in Gaza. Other films address the physical and psychological effects of violence on children, specifically as a result of the intifada. Another major area of focus over the last two years has been the issue of women’s rights. Women Rights are Human Rights tells the story of victims of domestic violence and explains the different aspects of violence against women in the Palestinian society. The Center also produces films that address the problems of water pollution, fishermen, youth, drug abuse, mental health, and mental illness.
The Video Center’s films have been shown in workshops and conferences and used in training throughout Palestine. In July 1997, the video team received an award for Near to Death at the Cairo Third International Festival for Television and Radio. Palestinian Television aired Little Hands, as did a television station in Italy. Currently negotiations are taking place to air more of the Center’s films on Palestinian Television.
The Video Center has filmed conferences, workshops, ceremonies, demonstrations, and interviews. The team is working with two independent filmmakers on documentaries concerning the problems faced by youth and by returnees in adapting to life in Gaza. Further, the Center has been documenting oral histories of refugees uprooted from their homes in 1948.

Over the next three years, the Video Center will produce two series of films dealing with mental health and mental illness. One series will consist of seventeen short (10-15 minute) films to be used by the different departments and projects of the GCMHP for training of both GCMHP staff and health professionals receiving advanced training in mental health. The second series of films is intended for public consumption in order to raise community awareness about mental health issues, overcome the stigma attached to mental illness, and assist people who are coping with psychological disorders – their own or those of friends and family.

The Gaza Video Center has played an important role in the work of the GCMHP during the last two years. With an aggressive strategy and an enthusiastic, dedicated staff, it will continue to provide Gaza with even more documentaries in order to disseminate necessary information about mental health issues and psycho-social problems in Palestinian society.

Little Hands

Tragically, child abuse is far from rare. Children are beaten, cut, burned, and scarred through physical aggression and maltreatment. They also face sexual and emotional abuse. While these forms of child abuse are common and recognized all over the world, certain forms of abuse go unnoticed. One such form is the tragic phenomena of child labor, recently on the rise here in Gaza.

More than 50% of the population in Gaza are children younger than 15 years of age. Sharing the abject conditions of life in Gaza with the rest of society, children also have particular hardships of their own. For the seven years of the intifada children were deprived of proper health and education services – basic needs of children everywhere. In addition, they faced the full force of Israeli military violence, which meant beatings, arrests, tear gas, house raids, live ammunition. This long-term traumatic treatment led to severe psychosocial disturbances.

Before the intifada, Palestinian children used to hear endless stories from their parents about their experiences of defeat. They saw keys to the homes from which their grandparents were uprooted. Hating the feelings of powerlessness and helplessness all around them, they refused to depend on their parents and decided to take the initiative and act on their own to recover their lost dignity. They wanted to prove that they could succeed where their parents and grandparents had failed. Now the intifada is over but the energy remains, and children feel that they must work because again the adults are unable to afford for the lives of their children and families.

During the intifada, the highest social value was accorded to resisting the Israeli occupation. As children played a central role in this resistance, they identified themselves with the image of power and were recognized as heroes. Not to lose that heroic identity and to maintain their psychological well being, children have adapted to the social value that has come to play an increasingly important role since 1993: material value. In Gaza today, the most important thing is to find a job and make money in order to survive. Money means power and children don’t want to lose the power which enabled them to achieve their self-actualization. Thus, they enter the labor market in search of social power.

Of course, these psychological determinants have to be understood within the socioeconomic context of the post-Oslo period. With the closure of the Gaza Strip in March 1993, and the economic siege beginning in February 1996, tens of thousands of Palestinian laborers were deprived of access to their work in Israel. As a result, unemployment rates in Gaza skyrocketed, reaching their peak at 70% of the Palestinian labor force. Accordingly, many Palestinian families began pushing their children to work in order to achieve a small income for the family. A study of child labor carried out by UNICEF examined 300 Palestinian children and showed that 52% of the children were forced into work by their families and that 44% of them began work only after Israel imposed the economic siege. The relationship between child labor and the socioeconomic conditions in Gaza is very clear.

Palestinian children suffer the effects of peace as they did the effects of war.
