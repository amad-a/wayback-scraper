1996-1997 Report / Third International Conference
 
Third International Conference
“Health and Human Rights”
13-15 October 1997
 
The Gaza Community Mental Health Programme held its third international conference, “Health and Human Rights,” October 13-15, 1997 in the Rashad El-Shawwa Cultural Center in Gaza City, Palestine. The conference brought together over 400 health professionals, scholars, and activists from 24 countries around the world to discuss the relationship between health, human rights, democracy and other ideals which contribute to the individual self and community spirit.
In his welcoming speech, Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj, Director of the Gaza Community Mental Health Programme and Commissioner General of the Palestinian Independent Commission for Citizens’ Rights, referred to the nascent experience of Palestinian democracy and the ongoing struggle to secure respect for Palestinian human rights. He emphasized the need for Palestinians to press ahead with building democracy and strengthening dialogue with the Palestinian Authority (PA).

Dr. Kamal El-Shrafi, head of the Monitoring and Human Rights Committee of the Palestinian Legislative Council (PLC), then opened the conference by raising the question of how Palestinians can move from backwardness and subordination toward pluralism and democracy. Insisting that the PA, while not a fully sovereign state, must abide by international standards of human rights, Dr. El-Shrafi detailed the close relationship between health and human rights and community development and poverty alleviation.

  Following the remarks of Dr. El-Sarraj and Dr. El-Shrafi, Dr. Jonathan Fine, Former Executive Director of Physicians for Human Rights, opened the plenary session with a paper on The Unfinished Task: Global Campaigning for Health and Human Rights. Dr. Fine asserted that over 1.5 billion people are suffering from degrading poverty throughout the world and that some of them could be found only one kilometer from the conference site. While governments neglect the poor, health professionals should support every national health professional group committed to advancing human rights, demonstrate unity and strength in asserting the universality of their deepest beliefs, let the world community know their vision, and consider what form a global movement for human rights will take.

Dr. James Welsh, President of the Medical Department of Amnesty International spoke on Doctors Campaigning Against Human Rights Violations. Dr. Welsh declared that medical professionals can help advance human rights through access to prisoners and those at risk, use of medical skills to determine incidents of torture, influence, and ethics. He then argued that medical professionals should protest more constructively, create greater awareness of human rights, render practical assistance in working with victims, and educate others more fully on crucial human rights issues.
 

 “The content of the meeting was stimulating, the arrangements extremely
effective, and the opportunities to discuss important human rights issues
were many.”
Dr. James Welsh, Coordinator, 
Medical Program, Amnesty International
 
In her discussion of The United States, Human Rights, and the Myth of the ‘Honest Broker’,
Dr. Nancy Murray of the Middle East Justice Network and the Bill of Rights Education Project, dispelled the myth that the US government acts as an ‘honest’ broker in the Middle East. She expressed concern that the U.S. is interested only in meeting Israeli security requirements and in keeping the talks going rather than in working toward the fulfillment of agreements.
Three concurrent workshop sessions were held in the morning: Perspectives on Human Rights, Women’s Rights in Palestine, and Children’s Rights. In the afternoon, six workshops were held: Medical Ethics: The Case of Israel, Rehabilitating Survivors of Human Rights Violations, Torture, Human Rights in Palestine, Forensic Psychiatry, and Rehabilitating Survivors of Human Rights Violations.

The second day Plenary Session was marred by the absence of Dr. Mustafa El-Barghouthi of the Union of Palestinian Medical Relief Committees. He was prevented by the Israeli authorities from traveling to the conference in Gaza. Other Palestinians denied access were: Dr. Ahmad Baker, Khalil Tuma (translator), Rana Nashashibi, Reema Shwaiki, Khalida Jarrar, Khalid Batrawi, Ahmed Sayyad, and Vivian Khamis. Such absences harmed the representation of West Bank Palestinians at such a very successful conference. In addition, several Israelis were preventedfrom entering Gaza, despite the fact that the GCMHP rigorously followed the procedure for obtaining permits. These Israelis were: Lisa Fliegel, Charles Lenchner, Diana Mardi, and Michal Schwartz. The Gaza Community Mental Health Programme (GCMHP) and conference participants condemned the intransigence of Israeli authorities involved in this matter. Freedom of travel is a basic human right that is being withheld by the Israeli authorities on a variety of pretexts.

In the Plenary Session, Professor Bent Sorensen, Vice-Chairman of the UN Committee against Torture, presented a paper on The UN Convention against Torture and the Case of Israel. In a scathing indictment of the ongoing Israeli policy of torture, Professor Sorensen detailed Israeli violations of the UN Convention against Torture and denounced the Landau Commission report that allowed the use of torture under the euphemism “moderate physical pressure” against detainees.

Dr. Adriaan Van Es of the International Federation of Health and Human Rights Organizations, delivered a speech entitled UN Special Rapporteurs on Health Professionals and Human Rights. Dr. Van Es expressed the concern that health professionals risk a great deal in reporting human rights violations, but there is a real need to overcome professional apathy and self-censorship. As a result, health professionals need legal protection and strong organizational backing, which a UN rapporteur could help meet.
 

 “The conference was of high quality, which impressed us deeply, 
and gave us a deeper insight into the daily realities of health 
professionals in Palestine and of the Palestinian population in general.”
Dr. Adriaan Van Es,
International Federation of Health and 
Human Rights Organizations
 
Dr. Rami Heilbronn of the Medical Foundation for the Care of Victims of Torture spoke powerfully and movingly during a speech entitled Bystanding and Whistle-blowing. He discussed the fragmentation of Jewish identity over the last 100 years. Israel, he declared, is an ethnocentric Jewish national state established to solve the persecution of Jews in Europe and cultural problems pertaining to secular Jewish identity. It solved neither. Furthermore, the suffering caused by Israel to Palestinians is an inevitable outcome of Jewish ethnocentrism. In a moving tribute, Heilbronn discussed the case of Mordechai Vanunu, an Israeli nuclear technician who gave secret information about the Israeli nuclear program to the London Sunday Times, for which he was kidnapped, arrested, and sentenced to 18 years in solitary confinement. Dr. Heilbronn argued that Vanunu moved beyond his ethnocentric perspective into an anthropocentric identity and consciousness when he blew the whistle on the secrets of the Dimona nuclear facility in Israel. According to Heilbronn, he has transcended loyalty to an ethnic group to a loyalty to humankind.
 
 “There was a genuineness, the feeling of solidarity, and not much
of that feeling of voyeurism, people coming to see how lucky they
are, people coming to wallow in the pleasure, to tell their friends,
“I’ve been to Gaza! I’ve been to Gaza! We are the rescuers...” 
The papers were meaningful and substantial, and most importantly,
it was not a kind of theoretical reflection.”
Dr. Rami Heilbronn,
Medical Foundation for the Care of Victims of Torture, UK
 
Three concurrent workshops were held in the morning: Mental Health and Human Rights, Violations of Women’s Rights, and Rehabilitating Survivors of Human Rights Violations. Six workshops were held in the afternoon: Mental Health and Human Rights, The Violation of Children’s Rights, Public Education Strategies, Preventing Torture, The Media and Human Rights, and The Violation of Children’s Rights.
The third morning Plenary Session brought together a distinguished panel of health professionals, scholars, and social activists. Psychiatrist and veteran African National Congress member
Dr. Freddy Reddy delivered a presentation on Suppression of Human Rights: Psychopathology in which he asserted that over the years doctors have contributed to tremendous wrongs, such as sterilization and dubious theories about minority groups. Linking the experiences of South Africa and the Gaza Strip, Dr. Reddy demonstrated that almost all people in Gaza have suffered from violations of their human rights. Palestinian children were the main victims and still suffer from Post Traumatic Stress Disorder. The treatment of personal suffering resulting from trauma involves a long process, but doctors and health personnel have an obligation to help. Another ongoing problem for Gazans is unemployment as it destroys self-respect and leads to despondency. He asserted, however, that people will only take so much before they rise up against the suppression of their rights.
Dr. Juliet Van Eenwyk of the Washington State Department of Health spoke on Epidemiology and Human Rights. She asserted that epidemiology can be used to improve health when people have been deprived of their human rights. She then outlined a number of examples in which epidemiology can help improve basic health conditions.

Dr. Khadija Safwat of the Middle Eastern and African Research Centre - Wales spoke on States and People under Siege: The Political Economy of Collective Siege and Human Rights. She declared that all states are under some form of siege because of the reproduction of capital, the lack of loyalty to the state, and the cross-boundary nature of the flow of capital. Looking at the situation today as a series of sieges, Dr. Safwat stated that Israel is an expensive ghetto that costs the West and the US a great deal. As Jewish contributions to Israel start to decline, Israel may develop a sense of coming under siege. As a result, it may be less willing to inflict a siege on the Palestinian economy, although it will have to compete economically with the Palestinian entity more and more.

The final speaker of the Plenary Session was Dr. Abdel Aziz El-Rantisi of the Islamic University. Speaking on Islam and Human Rights, Dr. El-Rantisi stated that ethics are to protect human dignity and that Islam is the best means by which to protect human rights. Under Islam, man can vote and attain public posts based on qualifications and not corruption. Dr. El-Rantisi proceeded to discuss the protections offered by Islam to members of minority religions and declared that the best struggle is in speaking truth in front of an unjust leader. During the question and answer session El-Rantisi stated that the Islam he had spoken of was the one sent by God and agreed that there are currently no good examples of Islamic governments for Palestinians to follow.

An Awards Ceremony was held after the Plenary Session to honor people and organizations that have contributed to the development of the GCMHP and to the defense of Palestinian health and human rights. The recipients of the awards were: Dr. Haidar Abdel Shafi, Ruchama Marton, Inge Genefke, Barbara Harrell-Bond, David Henley, Khader Shkirat, Rami Heilbronn, the Medical Foundation for the Care of Victims of Torture, the Union of Palestinian Medical Relief Committees, Lea Tzemel, the Public Committee Against Torture in Israel, and Federico Allodi.

Three concurrent workshops were held in the morning: Children, Public Education Strategies, and Women’s Rights. Three workshops were held in the afternoon: Rehabilitating Survivors of Human Rights Violations, Mental Health and Human Rights, and Ethics and Torture.

In the closing ceremony, Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj sounded a call for the release of Prof. Fat’hi Suboh.* In strident words, he invited the PA to base its strength on respect for law and human rights; “only then,” he said, “would we gladly sacrifice all we had for the Authority”. In the closing speech,

Riyad El-Za’noun, the Palestinian Minister of Health, called the participants “prophets”, and charged them with the mission of returning to their homelands and resounding the call for justice and the cry of injustice on behalf of the continuing Palestinian struggle. He closed with a moving tribute to his mother, an 89-year-old woman who weeps for the Palestinians massacred at El-Aqsa and Hebron as well as for the Israeli victims of suicide bombings. “The Israelis have mothers too,” she says. Za’noun related that when his mother asked him who the conference attendees were, he told her, “They are people like you, who weep when Palestinians die, who weep when Israelis die, and who rejoice when peace arrives”. “Tell them,” his mother replied, “Um Salim is with them”.
After the closing ceremony, Dr. Nabil Sha’ath, PA Minister of Planning and International Cooperation, attended the closing reception. He gave a speech to the participants in which he emphasized the sincere efforts of the PA to improve the health and human rights situation in Palestine. While describing the conference as a laoccasion, Dr. Sha’ath assured that the PA will take the recommendations and ideas presented in the conference into consideration.
 
In addition, a group of participants met President Yasser Arafat and conveyed to him their support for the Palestinian people in face of the Israeli human rights violations. Moreover, they stressed the importance of the PA adopting the principles of democracy and human rights as the basis of building a Palestinian civil society. Specifically, the delegation asked the President to release Prof. Fat’hi Suboh immediately. President Arafat thanked the participants for coming to Gaza, expressed his support for the respect of human rights, and stated that instructions were issued to the security branches to stop torture and abide by the law. Finally, he promised that improving the human rights situation in the Palestinian areas will be a priority for the PA and promised that Prof. Suboh would be taken to court if charged.
 
 “For those of us who have been in the struggle for many years,
we are comforted by this gathering; and for those of us who are 
just beginning to struggle, we are given inspiration. That has been
the real beauty of this conference. It has embraced people from 
all ages and all countries, all orientations, who have come 
together to share their ideas.”  
Dr. John R. Van Eenwyk,
University of Washington Medical School, USA
 
* Prof. Suboh was detained on 2 July 1997 for asking his students to answer a question about corruption in the PA. He was released on 26 November 1997.
