New Departments for 1998!!

January 1, 1998 will witness the birth of two new departments within the GCMHP – the Projects’ Funding Department (PFD) and the Information Technology Department (ITD). In addition, a Physiotherapy Unit will be created as part of the Project for the Rehabilitation of Victims of Human Rights Violations.

 
* Projects’ Funding Department (PFD) *

With the establishment of the Projects’ Funding Department, the GCMHP hopes to begin relieving its dependence on donor funding. While organizing fund-raising activities and assisting the funding of current activities, the main task of the PFD will be to initiate self-sustaining, income generating projects. With the goal of attaining a reasonable level of self-sufficiency over the next 3-4 years, the GCMHP will begin investing in private sector activities and using any profits to help fund GCMHP projects. The PFD will develop strategies, conduct market research, and coordinate implementation of GCMHP investment projects.

 

* Information Technology Department (ITD) *

In 1998, the Information Technology Department will launch the GCMHP into cyberspace. A primary task for the ITD will be designing, maintaining, and upgrading a state-of-the-art Internet website for the GCMHP (http://www.gcmhp.net). With links to related sites around the world and information about the Programme and its activities, the website will create a space for the GCMHP in the growing global information network. The ITD will also oversee the GCMHP computer information system, create a network to facilitate communication within the Programme, install modem connections at the clinics, maintain and upgrade the hardware, and provide technical assistance and necessary training for GCMHP employees.

 

* Physiotherapy Unit *

As part of the Project for the Rehabilitation of Victims of Human Rights Violations, the GCMHP will break new ground in 1998 by pioneering the junction of mental health and physiotherapy. The GCMHP Physiotherapy Unit will manage cases of ex-political prisoners with physical disabilities due to torture and conduct public awareness campaigns about the physical consequences of torture. Integrated into the three GCMHP clinics, the physiotherapist and assistant physiotherapist will provide manual treatment (massage, hot-pack, and cold-pack), ultrasound treatment, and paraffin oil baths to patients with stiffness or weakness of the extremities, pain in the lower back or neck, or tendon problems. Understanding the interdependence of the physical and mental effects of torture, the GCMHP hopes that the treatment of patients’ physical suffering will facilitate the treatment of their psychological suffering.

The GCMHP is very excited about these three new departments. We hope that our treatment, training, and research activities will benefit tremendously from the support that they will provide.
