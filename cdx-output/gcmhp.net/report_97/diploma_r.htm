Postgraduate Diploma in Community Mental Health
 
The Post Graduate Diploma in Community Mental Health, coordinated by the Training Department of the GCMHP, is the first of its kind in the Middle East: a two-year, culturally sensitive, community-based course designed to encourage professionals to explore theoretical concepts and practical applications of community mental health and human rights.
The first year of the course provides participants with training in state of the art theories, princ, and strategies of treatment and prevention. Under the guidance of international and local trainers, students complete ten core modules: Child/Adolescent Mental Health; Social Psychology; Clinical Psychiatry; Communication and Counseling; Culture and Mental Health; Trauma, Stress and Coping; Clinical Psychology; Research Methodology; Community Mental Health; and Mental Health Education. During the first year, students are also gradually exposed to clinical experience, interacting with clients to offer support and emotional containment without the demands of a specified therapeutic encounter. These experiences are aimed at facilitating the integration of theoretical and clinical work and the development of professional identity.
During the second year, participants receive a series of supervised clinical placements to explore the application of their knowledge. Students carry an outpatient caseload of 4-6 patients and provide therapy according to the needs of the patient. Over the year, the students also complete six, two-month rotations. Four are core rotations conducted by all of the students in the areas of child/adolescent, adult, community, and family; two are elective rotations, in which students choose to focus on women, geriatric, group, or other areas that interest them. The program culminates with the completion of a final project, in which the students integrate their theoretical and clinical training by writing about a clinical experience or community issue in which they were involved.
The course is designed to encourage participants to explore theoretical concepts in community mental health and human rights in addition to their practical applications. Wide exposure to clinical experience enables participants to discover their particular interest in mental health and helps the transition from theoretical class-based study to skilled and practical application within the community.
Placed firmly within the philosophy of community-based mental health, the Diploma emphasizes the shift from segregated and self-contained mental health care systems within institutions to community-based therapy and community involvement in health related issues. As such, the course seeks to shift psychiatric therapy away from traditional Freudian concentration on the individual and his unconscious motivational conflicts to move closer towards an emphasis on social interpersonal care and the role of a person’s environment. It also emphasizes the change in the role of mental health professionals from being providers of mental health care to facilitators of care, thus enabling individuals to solve their problems using the resources of family and community institutions such as schools, mosques, etc. Finally, the Diploma hopes to expose students to notions of human rights and to highlight the links between mental health and human rights. No course in mental health would be complete without an analysis of human rights and the role that they play within the community.
In February 1997, nine male and three female students began the Postgraduate Diploma: one physician, two social workers, and nine nurses. All theoretical courses will end in February 1998, when the students will begin their practical training in the following institutions: Palestinian Red Crescent Society, Child Development Center, Governmental Psychiatric Hospital, National Association for the Rehabilitation of the Handicapped, Right to Life Society for Children with Down’s Syndrome, Al-Wafa Association for the Elderly, Deaf Children Center, and all three GCMHP clinics. Also in 1998, a second group of twelve students (nine males, three females) will begin the first year of the course: five physicians, three nurses, three psychologists, and one rehabilitation specialist.
The Diploma course is coordinated by a Partnership Council, including the GCMHP, the Islamic University of Gaza, and Oslo University-Norway. Seven international universities contribute to the Postgraduate Diploma Project by providing instructors for the various modules: Edinburgh University-UK, Flinders University-Australia, Oslo University-Norway, University of California at San Francisco-USA, Tel Aviv University-Israel, Tunis University-Tunisia, and Utrecht University-the Netherlands. Each of these universities also has a representative on the Academic Board of the Diploma. While each university has declared its involvement in and expressed its enthusiasm for the Diploma Project, the Training Department is currently working to formalize links between the universities and the GCMHP.
In August 1997, the Academic Board held its first meeting in Utrecht to evaluate the diploma and set a timetable for 1998. Feedback from the professors demonstrated that both the student’s enthusiasm and the level of professional and administrative support were extremely high. Many trainers noticed, however, that students were often exhausted by the demands of work, because many would work in the morning and take classes in the afternoon. In response, the Board decided to recruit students to training posts and hire them for their period of study. By including training posts and making the Diploma a full time project, the GCMHP hopes to relieve some of the pressure on participants and to facilitate their active participation in the learning process. The changes will be implemented in 1998.
