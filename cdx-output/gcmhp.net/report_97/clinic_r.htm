Introduction
For fifty years, the constant devastation and violence directed against the Palestinian people have affected not only the social, economic, and political structures of the Gaza community, but also the psychological well-being of a vast number of Palestinian people. Mass arrests, torture, beatings, tear gas, home demolitions, murders, violent confrontations, and house raids generated feelings of helplessness, victimization, and humiliation. The trauma that they instilled has created severe, long-term psychological damage.
 
The GCMHP was established in 1990 to address the mental health needs of a devastated community in the midst of massive social upheaval. Clinical work remains the heart of the GCMHP’s sustained efforts to heal the wounds and meet the continued and changing needs of the Palestinian community.
Over the last two years, the clinical staff has become increasingly specialized through advanced training and experience. With five psychiatrists, four general practitioners, four social workers, ten psychologists, three psychiatric nurses, four staff nurses, and one occupational therapist, the GCMHP has adopted a specialized case management process that allows for differential handling of cases for women, children, and victims of human rights violations. Upon receiving a referral, a member of the team conducts a comprehensive clinical interview in order to begin assessment. If necessary, home, school, and work visits, as well as physical, psychological, or social evaluations are conducted to complete the assessment. The assessment is then presented at a team discussion, during which the team makes diagnoses and creates treatment plans. At that point, a case manager takes responsibility for the case, providing and ensuring treatment, conducting following-ups, making home visits, and carrying out evaluations.
The benefits of this process are clear from the tremendous increase in the success of our clinical work over the last two years. While the number of new cases remains constant at nearly 1200 per year, the number of follow up visits has doubled to over 10,000 per year. In addition, we are losing far less drop-outs per year.
In 1998, the GCMHP clinical teams will expand with the addition of a physiotherapy unit. Two physiotherapists will work with the teams in the different clinics in order to provide further services to GCMHP patients, especially victims of human rights violations.
With a combination of psychological, family, and community interventions, the GCMHP clinical teams attempt to provide a supportive environment in which healing can take place. While performing classical psychological work, the GCMHP firmly roots its therapy in a culturally sensitive, community based approach, adapting Western approaches to the needs of the Palestinian society.
 
New Cases:
The Gaza Community Mental Health Programme saw 1097 new cases in 1996 and 1153 new cases in 1997.
 
Referrals:
The GCMHP still receives the vast majority of its referrals from the family or the individual (78.7%). While over the last two years the GCMHP has received referrals from an increasing number of sources, there has been a decrease in the number of referrals coming from some sources that previously referred cases to the GCMHP clinics. One notable change is that in 1997 the Women’s Empowerment Project began referring several cases to the GCMHP clinics. Another significant development has been the great increase in the number of referrals from community workers over the last two years. Such an increase demonstrates the success of GCMHP training programs that have provided community workers with the knowledge and skills to detect and help prevent mental illness.
 
 
Demographic distribution:
 
Age:
The progressive increase in the number of children (under 18 years) seen in GCMHP clinics has leveled off during 1996 and 1997. While children made up only 29.3% of new cases in 1991 and 40% in 1994, the percentage has become steady at just over 45%, with 500 new cases in 1996 and 501 in 1997.
 
 
Gender: 

The GCMHP received 601 male patients and 496 female patients in 1996; 639 male and 514 female patients in 1997.  Despite cultural taboos that prevent women from seeking mental health care, the ratio of male (55%) to female (45%) patients in GCMHP clinics reflects the gender ratio that exists throughout Palestinian society. This demonstrates the success of the GCMHP’s efforts to reach Palestinian women. 
 
 
 
Refugees and Citizens: 

The percentage of citizens among new cases has increased substantially over the last two years.  While 247 (22.4%) new cases in 1996 were citizens, that number climbed to 329 (28.5%) in 1997. 
 
 
Diagnosis:
 The GCMHP clinics continue to see a decrease in the number of cases with organic disorders. This is partially explained by the fact that many chronic cases are now being referred to other servers. The increase in mood disorders stems from rising depression associated with the deteriorating social and economic conditions. There has also been an increase in disorders usually first diagnosed in infancy, childhood, and adolescence, referred to as ‘childhood’ in the chart.

Outcome:
The outcome of treatment provided in the GCMHP clinics has improved substantially over the last two years.  The number of patients who have been cured or whose status has improved continues to rise steadily.  At the same time, the drop out rate continues to fall as do the number of patients whose conditions have relapsed.

Follow Up Cases
The total number of follow-up patients who received treatment in 1996 was 1923 with an average of 4.0 visits per patient. During 1997, there were 2200 patients with an average of 4.6 visits per patient. Of these, 58.2% were male and 41.8% female in 1996; 59.9% male and 40.1% female in 1997. In 1996, 41.1% of the patients were under 18 years of age. In 1997, this proportion stayed roughly the same as 39.1% of the patients were under 18.
Therapy:
Counseling remains the most common form of therapy provided at the GCMHP. Most patients also receive supportive therapy, especially chronic cases such as those with schizophrenia and other psychotic disorders. In addition, the staff provides cognitive therapy for patients with depressive conditions and cognitive-behavioral therapy for those with PTSD. Behavioral therapy is used mainly for patients with obsessive-compulsive disorder and phobias, while children receive different kinds of therapy, mainly play and behavioral therapy. Listed in the chart as ‘others’ are the remaining forms of therapy provided, including insight and purposive psychotherapy.

Occupational Therapy:
The Occupational Therapy Unit was established in 1991 at the Jabalia clinic. Occupational therapy enables patients to function as independently as possible in their daily life activities and working environment. During the OT sessions, patients develop their skills in recreational activities, such as sports, and vocational activities, such as wood-working, drawing, painting, and carpet weaving. The most common patients are those suffering from chronic conditions such as schizophrenia or chronic depression.
During 1997, the Occupational Therapy Unit conducted 937 individual and group therapy sessions: 779 for male and 158 for female patients. The outcome of these sessions showed positive results, as the vast majority of clients either improved or had their conditions controlled

EEG:
Until 1996, both the Gaza and Khan Younis clinics were equipped with Electro-Encephalo-Graphy (EEG) machines.  In April 1996, however, the EEG machine in Khan Younis broke down and the unit was closed.  In addition, during 1996 an EEG unit opened at the governmental psychiatric hospital.  As a result, 1996 and 1997 have seen a decrease in the number of patients requesting EEG: while 481 patients were seen in 1995, only 201 patients were seen in 1996 and 233 in 1997.  Just over 50% of them suffer from epilepsy.
 
Pharmacy:
Although the GCMHP does not follow a medicine-oriented approach, the medical teams do prescribe psychotropic drugs to some patients, such as chronic cases and those requiring medication for a short period at the beginning of therapy. To ensure that patients receive the apprmedication at an affordable price, each clinic has a pharmacy. Due to the deterioration of the economic situation, clients receive medication free of charge. While during 1994 and 1995, approximately 30% of the prescriptions were provided free of charge, in 1996 the GCMHP provided 903 out of 2245 prescriptions (40.2%) free. In 1997, that number increased to 1523 out of 2672 prescriptions (57%). Those that paid for their medication received it at cost price.
