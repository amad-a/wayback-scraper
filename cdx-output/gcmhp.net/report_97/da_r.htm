1996-1997 Report / Drug Abuse
 
 
Drug Abuse

One of the often unrecognized results of the Israeli occupation has been the spread of drug abuse throughout Palestine. Hundreds of thousands of Palestinians working inside Israel have been surrounded by a subculture engaged in large-scale drug use. In addition, the Israeli occupation authorities turned a blind eye to the growth of dealers and smugglers and encouraged the dissemination of illicit drugs within the West Bank and Gaza Strip in what some people suspected to be a policy designed to destroy the Palestinian individual and society.

The intifada shattered the network of collaboration between Israeli and Palestinian drug dealers and Palestinian militants induced the fear of physical, social, and psychological violence into both dealers and users. As a result, drug use in Palestine decreased considerably and many addicts sought help. Upon its establishment in 1990, the GCMHP began providing treatment for drug addicts. When compared with drug programs elsewhere, our approach to treatment has been very successful. Of our 250 patients, we have had a success rate of 44% (11.2% cured and 32.8% improved), 29.1% no change, 10.8% relapsed, 7.2% dropped out, 4.8% closed files, 3% died, and 1.2% referred elsewhere.

The Oslo process has resulted in drastic changes in the context of drugs in Palestine. The fear of intifada militants is gone; the network of Israeli and Palestinian dealers has been rebuilt; the Egyptian laws being enforced by the PA in Gaza do not adequately address drug abuse or distribution; the lack of a forensic lab makes investigations for drugs impossible; soaring unemployment, rapidly dying dreams of peace, political oppression, widespread PTSD, continued suffering, anger, frustration, and despair have aggravated the inclination to reject society and take refuge in drugs. While the strong family and religious structures in Gaza and the stigma of shame attached to drugs still provide a check on the rise of drug use, reports indicate that well over 5,000 people in Gaza abuse drugs.

One major consequence of these changes has been a sharp decline in the number of addicts seeking treatment. As a result, over the last two years the Drug Abuse Committee at the GCMHP has increasingly engaged in community outreach aimed at prevention. Most of the Committee’s activities are directed towards young men, the sector of Palestinian society most vulnerable to drug addiction. In order to reach the largest number of people, the Committee coordinates with the Ministry of Youth and Sport, the National Guidance Commission, and the Ministry of Social Affairs, as well as clubs, universities, mosques, political parties, and other institutions that engage young Palestinian men.

The outreach activities of the Drug Abuse Committee over the last two years include:

  • Public meetings: The Drug Abuse Committee has held 24 public meetings, attended by 1700 people from the local community, to address drug abuse, its consequences, treatment, and prevention. Two volunteer support committees were established from the participants of the workshops. These committees consist of religious leaders, teachers, community leaders, nurses, police officers, advocates, and other members of the local community interested in facilitating our work.
  • Workshops: The Committee organized three workshops, “Preparing young leaders,” “Drug Abuse: Causes and Methods of Prevention,” and “Drug Abuse: Reality and Solutions.” Attended by hundreds of representatives of local organizations and institutions, these workshops provoked discussion of critical issues related to drug abuse. In addition, members of the Committee participated in workshops organized by other institutions.
  • Lectures: At public meetings and workshops, members of the Committee have given lectures on topics such as “Drug Addiction and its Risk to Palestinian Society,” “The Reasons for Drug Abuse,” “Drug Abuse: Its Causes, Stages and Effects,” “The Reasons & Psycho-social Effects of Drug Addiction,” “Drug Addiction: Its Definition, Symptoms and Social Effects on Students,” and “The Effects of Drug Addiction on the Child and the Family.”
  • Media: Members of the Drug Abuse Committee have published four articles in local newspapers, given interviews for four television programs, and taken part in five televised seminars on drug abuse. Further, the Committee compiled and edited a manual for drug abuse that will be published in 1998.
  • Provision of aid: In May 1997, the Drug Abuse Committee, in coordination with the World Food Program, began providing aid to the families of addicts involved in treatment.
Drug abuse remains a serious issue that Palestine will be forced to confront for years to come. Only with a major transformation of the prevailing social, political, economic, and psychological contexts in Palestine will the issue be resolved. In the meantime, the GCMHP is actively engaged in helping the Palestinian community check the rising tide of drug abuse through our emphasis on prevention.
