Human Rights

  A central characteristic of the Palestinian experience over the last fifty years has been the systematic violation of virtually every internationally recognized human right by the Israeli government. Dispossession, forced migration, occupation, economic siege, curfew, closure, confiscation, deportation, house demolition, night raids, tear gas, beatings, “rubber” bullets… One of the most overt and destructive Israeli abuses has been a thirty-year campaign of massive imprisonment and systematic torture. Over 535,000 Palestinians have been detained since the beginning of the occupation in 1967; 175,000 during the seven years of the intifada alone (Solidarity International, 1997). In 1987, the Israeli Landau Commission recommended the use of “moderate physical pressure” during interrogations, thus making Israel the only state in the world to legally sanction torture. Tens of thousands of Palestinians have suffered brutal treatment at the hands of Israeli interrogators, leading to disastrous psychological effects, not only on the individual victims, but also on the Palestinian community as a whole.

With its establishment in 1990, the GCMHP realized the need to address these effects and has always placed an emphasis on rehabilitating ex-prisoners and victims of human rights abuses. In 1994, the GCMHP created the Human Rights Committee to address the needs of the thousands of ex-political prisoners released in the aftermath of the Oslo agreement as well as those detained by the Palestinian Authority due to their criticism of the peace process or of the PA. New evidence of torture taking place within Palestinian Authority prisons as well as other abuses of human rights by the PA makes an emphasis on human rights all the more crucial.

In order to facilitate the work of the Human Rights Committee, the GCMHP worked throughout 1997 to initiate the Project for the Rehabilitation of Victims of Human Rights Violations in January 1998. With a framework and a three-year plan, the Project will concentrate on rehabilitating victims and decreasing the prevalence of human rights violations in Palestine. The new structure will improve the effectiveness of the GCMHP in addressing the pressing issue of human rights abuses.

The Human Rights Committee has four main objectives:

  • To improve the quality and quantity of treatment, counseling, and therapy provided by the GCMHP staff to survivors of human rights violations. The GCMHP staff receives continuous, advanced training in mental health and human rights including lectures by visiting professors, enrollment in the GCMHP Post Graduate Diploma in Community Mental Health, membership in a five-year GCMHP course in group therapy, pursuit of high degrees at foreign universities, and countless workshops and lectures organized in conjunction with the various committees, projects, and departments of the GCMHP.
  • To increase the capacity of rehabilitating survivors of human rights violations. During the last year, the Project has treated a total of 1272 direct and indirect victims of human rights abuses, including 225 new cases. With the new structure, the Programme will increase its capacity to address over 300 new cases annually. Using a professional, culturally sensitive approach, the GCMHP treats victims individually as well as with their families, the indirect victims of human rights abuses. Combining counseling with individual and group psychotherapy, occupational therapy, play therapy, and, beginning in 1998 physiotherapy, the teams of therapists, social workers, doctors, and psychiatrists address the particular needs of each case. In addition, to improve the quality of treatment, the rehabilitation teams receive a variety of supervision and evaluation from professional psychiatrists and psychotherapists.
  • To increase the support given to survivors of human rights violations by their institutions, enabling them to integrate survivors into society. Over the last two years, the Human Rights Committee members have delivered more than thirty lectures at public forums and participated in over thirty workshops held by different organizations in Gaza. In addition, the Committee has organized over ten workshops for members of the community, NGOs, and support groups, such as the Red Cross/Red Crescent, the YMCA, the Ex-Prisoners Rehabilitation Program, and the Zaqat Committee of the Ministry of the Awqaf. Issues addressed include coping with trauma, post traumatic stress disorder, conflict resolutions, family violence, violence against women, mental health and mental illness, psychosocial effects of torture, and violence in the Palestinian society.
  • To prevent the violation of human rights through training of police officers and social workers. During the coming year, the Human Rights Committee will place a new emphasis on prevention, the most effective method of dealing with human rights abuses. The staff will engage in: workshops and training about human rights for Palestinian police and members of the Palestinian Authority; media campaigns and lectures to raise public awareness about human rights; and training professionals in the early detection of mental health problems related to torture and other human rights violations.
In 1998, the GCMHP’s new physiotherapy unit will begin providing treatment to victims of human rights abuses. Physiotherapy helps create balance and harmony between body and mind as well as helping to heal certain physical problems, especially those related to muscles.
The GCMHP has helped thousands of victims deal with the effects of human rights abuses. It plays an active role in the growing campaign to end such abuses. While the human rights situation in Palestine remains precarious, the GCMHP hopes that its activities towards rehabilitating survivors of human rights violations can help to counter the continued abuses.


 
PHYSICIANS FOR HUMAN RIGHTS – USA HONORS DR. EYAD EL-SARRAJ
On 12 April 1997, Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj received a special award from Physicians for Human  
Rights, USA, an organization of health professionals, scientists, and concerned citizens.   
At the 10th Anniversary Celebration of PHR-USA, Dr. El-Sarraj was honored as a recipient of  
the First PHR Human Rights Award in recognition of “his unswerving commitment to health, 
human rights, and peace in the region.”  Along with Dr. El-Sarraj, US Senator Patrick Leahy 
was recognized for his leadership in advancing the call for an international ban on landmines and  
the Turkish Medical Association was honored for the courageous efforts of Turkish physicians to 
protect the health and human rights of the people of Turkey.  
 
 
 
Three Times in Six Months: The Arrests of Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj

In addition to his position as Director General of the Gaza Community Mental Health Programme, Dr Eyad El-Sarraj serves as Commissioner of the Palestinian Independent Commission for Citizens’ Rights. The PICCRis a state institution charged with monitoring and working towards the integration of human rights into Palestinian legislation and institutions.

The mandate of the PICCR establishes that it will work in an open, transparent relationship with the Palestinian Authority in order to promote respect for the rights of Palestinian citizens. During late 1995 and 1996, the PICCR encountered many difficulties in trying to cooperate with the PA. Repeated attempts to contact the Attorney General and the President regarding cases of death in detention, torture, and detention without trial went unanswered.

At a workshop on 6 December 1995, Dr. El-Sarraj voiced concern that Palestinian Attorney General Khaled El-Qidra presented a major obstacle to the fulfillment of PICCR tasks and responsibilities. The PA responded to Dr. El-Sarraj’s criticism by arresting him the following day. He was questioned, held in custody, interrogated, confronted by the Attorney General, and asked to sign a statement that he would never expose publicly or in any manner the PA, its symbols, or its members. Despite his refusal to sign, Dr. El-Sarraj was released after nine hours in detention.

Five months later, the Palestinian Authority again arrested Dr. El-Sarraj for criticizing its practices. On 18 May 1996, Dr. El-Sarraj was arrested after being quoted in an article in the New York Times. He was held for 48 hours on charges of “slandering” the Palestinian Authority. The State Security Court then extended his sentence for 15 days. Amnesty International, the Palestinian Centre for Human Rights, the Palestine Human Rights Information Centre, Al-Haq, Adameer Association, and many other human rights organizations condemned the arrest of Dr. El-Sarraj, considered him a “prisoner of conscience,” called for his release, and demanded protection for the freedom of expression. After spending 8 days in jail, Dr. El-Sarraj was released on 26 May. Asked to sign a statement that he would abide by the law, Dr. El-Sarraj added a handwritten note that he maintained his full freedom of expression. He was reminded that he was liable to re-arrest.

Two weeks later, on 9 June 1996, the PA arrested Dr. El-Sarraj for the third time in six-months after he sent a critical letter to President Arafat concerning an appearance by the President on CNN. At the prison, Dr. El-Sarraj was beaten, locked in a cell 120cm x 250cm for 17 days, denied visits by his family and lawyers, and charged with possession of hashish. During interrogations, one officer inquired about the letter that Dr. El-Sarraj had written to the President. When the Attorney General read about this in the proceedings, he angrily reprimanded the officer and told him he was not supposed to ask such questions. With no grounds for the possession charges, the PA charged Dr. El-Sarraj with assaulting the officer who beat him the day he was arrested. On the fifth day of his arrest Dr. El-Sarraj was taken to a military court, where he was sentenced to an additional 15 days in prison. Again, an international campaign emerged to secure the release of Dr. El-Sarraj. Organizations, activists, professionals, politicians, and leaders around the world condemned his imprisonment, called for his immediate release, and demanded that the Palestinian Authority respect the freedom of expression. On 26 June, all charges were dropped and Dr. El-Sarraj was released, after spending 17 days in jail.

The repeated arrests of Dr. El-Sarraj and lack of respect for his freedom of expression clearly demonstrate the context in which the GCMHP is forced to carry out its work. Such an atmosphere, in which fear prevents people from expressing their thoughts and feelings, makes it extremely difficult to conduct mental health work, especially rehabilitating victims of violence. Fortunately, the exposure that the arrests of Dr. El-Sarraj received has led to widespread realization of the urgency to protect human rights in Palestine.
