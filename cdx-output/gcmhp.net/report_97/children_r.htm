1996-1997 Report / Children
 
 
Children
 
Children remain one of the most vulnerable segments of the Palestinian population. The intifada, the struggle which brought Palestinian children worldwide recognition, also brought them immense suffering as the Israeli war machine attempted to brutally crush the uprising. Children were beaten, shot, arrested, and exposed to tear gas; they had their bones broken, their houses raided, their friends and families humiliated. The tremendous, lasting psychological damage that the Israeli soldiers inflicted upon Palestinian children has left them to cope with anxiety, depression, Post Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD), hyperactivity, bedwetting, sleep walking, night-terror and nightmares. As the Israeli imposed closure and continued intransigence suppresses Gaza’s attempts to crawl forward in the post-Oslo era, Palestinian children face new anxieties and stresses associated with harsh economic conditions and the despair resulting from unrealized dreams of peace.
Providing mental health care for the children of Gaza has always been a major concern of the GCMHP. In 1991, the GCMHP began working with children and teachers in kindergartens. In 1993, the Children’s Committee was formed to develop and advance the approach of the GCMHP towards improving the mental health status of children in the area. Through the integration of preventive and therapeutic work, the Children’s Committee continues its vital activity.

 
The Children’s Committee provided treatment to 502 new clients in 1997, with 65% referred by the family and 19% by community workers, and the other 16% by doctors, kindergartens, schools, and NGOs. The Children’s Committee staff, consisting of one psychiatrist, one general practitioner, six psychologists, two social workers, and one nurse, provide therapy to children through counseling, supportive therapy, play therapy, family therapy, individual dynamic and cognitive behavior psychotherapy. Nearly one-half of the treatment sessions during 1997 consisted of family based counseling.
In order to initiate contact with increasing numbers of children, the Committee works within the settings that structure most children’s activities: schools, summer camps, clubs, and kindergartens.
  • The Committee worked with 120 children at three summer camps in 1997, conducting sessions devoted to: true story-telling, in which children are asked to relate important events in their lives and to respond to the stories of others; role-playing, in which groups of children create roles for themselves and act them out; and free drawing, in which they are free to draw whatever they want. At the same time, children are asked to tell about their dreams.
  • The Committee visited 9 schools informally in 1996, working in close cooperation with teachers. In those visits, the staff met teachers, arranged for formal meetings, and conducted assessment and follow-up on cases of children referred to the GCMHP by the schools. In 1997, the staff conducted 43 visits to 13 schools, counseling teachers and assessing potential child cases. Finally, in 1997 the GCMHP received formal permission to visit 12 UNRWA schools. At these schools, the staff meets with teachers and conducts story-telling, role-playing, and free drawing sessions with groups of 35 children.
  • In February 1997, the UNRWA director asked the Children’s Committee to intervene in a crisis after a 12-year old student was beaten to death by her father. A team of two psychologists and two social workers conducted a debriefing process and treated the students’ post traumatic stress reactions.
  • During the first six months of 1997, the Children’s Committee visited 28 kindergartens, working with children, parents, and teachers to promote awareness of mental health issues. In kindergartens, both teachers and parents can be approached outside of the clinical setting, thus minimizing the stigma of psychiatric consultation. The staff interacts with children, educates teachers about how to deal with psychological problems, and conducts meetings (13 in the first half of 1997) with the children’s mothers, all of which allows for more questions about behavioral and psychological problems.
 
Over the last two years, the Children’s Committee has increased its outreach work intended to raise public awareness about mental health issues and to widen its network of referrals.
  • In 1997, the staff gave thirty lectures at public meetings on subjects such as bed wetting, child aggression, anxiety, phobia, trauma and children, children’s rights, play therapy, communicating with children, lying, normal and abnormal development, the effects of violence on children, children’s behavior problems, risk factors of conduct disorder in children, and the mental health of families.
  • The Children’s Committee made 42 visits in 1997 to other institutions working with children, such as The Child Development Center, El-Shorque Club, World Refugee Children, Canaan Institution, El-Wafa Rehabilitation Center, El-Helal Center, Save the Children, and the Child Educational Center. They provided workshops and coordinated activities, including case referrals and training.
  • The staff conducted two workshops for teachers on 15-16 March 1997 and 3-4 November 1997. The staff presented subjects such as the concept of communication, communication blocks, family communication, physical development, social development, and emotional development to the 35 teachers who participated.
 Finally, the Children’s Committee took part in the training offered by the Training Department of the GCMHP. Children’s Committee members participated in training activities such as courses, study days, workshops, seminars, and case conferences.
During 1998, the Children’s Committee intends to expand, adopting programs to train young adults about child development and to provide them with communications skills, sexual education, and other knowledge to promote effective parenting.

Children remain one of the most targeted groups in Palestinian society. Exposed to violence in the home and in the streets, forced to labor under difficult conditions, and surrounded by economic deprivation, Palestinian children face immense difficulties and severe trauma. The Children’s Committee at the GCMHP addresses that trauma, offering therapy and counseling to children, teachers, and child-care providers in order to help children overcome the many obstacles to their development so they can live a strong, healthy life.
 

CHILDREN’S DRAWINGS EXHIBITED IN JAPAN

Among the most important therapeutic techniques used by the Children’s Committee is 
“Free Drawing.”  At summer camps, kindergartens, and schools, the Committee gives 
children the opportunity to express their hopes, dreams, and fears through drawing. 
This provides an important outlet for children to release their emotions and communicate 
their traumas.  It also provides a means for the Children’s Committee staff to assess the 
mental health needs of children, both on an individual and a community level. 

Some of these drawings are now being exhibited throughout Japan in an effort to raise 
awareness of the situation of Palestinian children.  Peace on Earth, a Japanese NGO, 
has included ten drawings from the GCMHP collection, along with thirteen other 
drawings from Palestine, fifteen photos, a map, and Palestinian embroidery in the 
“Art Exhibition of Traumatic Children in Palestine.”  A six-day exhibition during 
December 1997 attracted over 800 visitors.  Throughout 1998, Peace on Earth 
will continue to hold the exhibition at various sites in Japan. 

The response so far has been impressive: 

“I think that Palestinian children bare hardships beyond imagination.” 

“We tend not to realize the meaning of ‘freedom’ until we are deprived of it. 
I think that the situation in Palestine gives Japanese an opportunity to reconsider 
what ‘freedom’ means.” 

“I hope and pray that those children’s trauma be cured as soon as possible.”
