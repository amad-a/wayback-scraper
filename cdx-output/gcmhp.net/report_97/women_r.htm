1996-1997 Report / Women’s Empowerment
 
Women’s Empowerment
 
Women in Palestinian society not only face the continued violence and hardships of foreign military occupation, they also confront the force of the patriarchal structure of Palestinian society - especially in Gaza, where the traditional family structure remains very powerful. During the intifada, many Palestinian women were direct victims of Israeli military violence – beaten, shot, arrested. In addition, they faced the psychological and emotional damage of having fathers, husbands, and brothers killed and imprisoned. Suddenly left on their own, women were confronted with a society that had not prepared them with the necessary life skills and education and restricted their personal freedoms. In the post-Oslo period, a strict closure strangles the economy and drives up unemployment rates while many thousands of severely tortured ex-prisoners try to cope with their traumatic experiences inside Israeli and Palestinian prisons. As a result, Palestinian society has seen a sharp rise in domestic and social violence, much of which is directed against Palestinian women.
The Women’s Empowerment Project at the GCMHP was established in 1994 to empower women through education, vocational training, therapy, legal counseling, leadership training, and community networking. Through its various components, the Project allows women to identify and address their most pressing community and personal needs, thus helping women victims manage their psychological problems and play an active role in Palestinian society.

While some governmental and non-governmental relief programs address the material needs of women, the lasting psychological effects of suffering and violence have not been tackled. The Women’s Empowerment Project seeks to address the psychological needs of women victims of violence through its individual, family, and group psychotherapy sessions. During 1997, the Project held 195 psychosocial counseling sessions. In addition to Project participants, 38 women and 4 children from the community received psychosocial counseling. Beyond counseling and therapy, Project psychologists, social workers, and community development leaders conduct home visits and organize support groups.

The primary goal of the Women’s Empowerment Project is to enable women to attain the knowledge and skills necessary to take on new roles in society. The Project provides several different types of educational and training opportunities for Palestinian women:

  • Education: Recognizing the power that knowledge imparts, the Project seeks to provide women with educational opportunities. Several women receive assistance to continue their education at the universities in Gaza. The Project also provides courses in computing, first , income-generation, and food production. In addition, daily lectures over the last year have addressed issues such as: Palestinian women’s role in the community; Women’s right to divorce; Women’s right to custody; Forms of violence: verbal, physical, sexual, and social; Women and personal status law; Violence against women and prevention; Family planning; and lectures concerning women’s health.
  • Vocational training: Through vocational training, the Project continues to provide women with the skills necessary to start income-generating projects and achieve some economic independence. Depending on their needs and interests, participants receive training in sewing and fashion design, embroidery, knitting, glass paintings, ceramics, cooking, video, photography, and framing.
  • Leadership training (Community development leaders): Adopting a “training the trainers” methodology, the Project held community leadership training in Rafah in 1996 and the Beach Camp in 1997. This training included modules on community health, counseling, first aid, conflict resolution, leadership skills, training of trainers, data collection, and small project management. Following their training, these leaders conducted needs assessment surveys to better understand women’s needs and, in response, developed and organized an advanced program to address those needs.
 At the conclusion of this training in 1997, the Women’s Empowerment Project established women’s centers in Rafah and the Beach Camp to be run by the trained community leaders. The centers provide psychosocial counseling, legal counseling, vocational training, legal literacy, and community awareness campaigns. Women in need of psychotherapy are referred to the other GCMHP clinics. In 1998, the Project will begin working toward the establishment of a center in the middle area of the Gaza Strip.
Another service provided to participants in the Women’s Empowerment Project is free legal counseling. During 1997, the Project conducted 90 legal counseling sessions and the legal advisor represented many cases in Sharia Courts.

The Women’s Empowerment Project is constantly expanding its community network of information and support. Through public meetings, workshops, community awareness campaigns, and several media outlets, the Project addresses issues of women’s rights as well as other cultural and social problems. During 1997, the Project held workshops that dealt with: violence against women, the killing of women by family members to preserve family honor, women’s rights to divorce, the law, its implementation, and the difficulties women face inside courts. In January 1997, as part of its community outreach work, the Project began coordinating with police to provide policewomen with understanding and skills to help them deal effectively with social problems in the local community. In addition, the Project coordinates activities with governmental and non-governmental organizations working with woman, health, and human rights.

Also in 1997, the GCMHP Women’s Empowerment Project began providing out-service training to people in the community. The Project organized two courses for child care providers, one course for Palestinian policewomen, and a rehabilitation program for women prisoners inside the Gaza central prison.

The position of women in Palestinian society remains particularly vulnerable. With a seemingly overwhelming array of forces aligned against them, over 500 Palestinian women have found support over the last two years in the services of the Women’s Empowerment Project. These services have helped many women overcome the effects of violence and play an active role in society. As the Women’s Empowerment Project reaches more and more women, its services become ever more significant in helping to affect the status of women in the Gaza community.
