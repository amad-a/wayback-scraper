Our War - the View from Bethlehem

by Nancy Murray

Standing on a roof in the West Bank town of Beit Jala which borders Bethlehem, I observed for several hours the latest communications between Israel and the Palestinians. On one side were Palestinians in olive groves firing rifles in the direction of the Israeli settlement of Gilo, their bullets mostly falling short of their target. On the other side, the Israeli Defense Force was responding with helicopter-launched guided missiles, a barrage of machine gun fire and exploding tank shells. Before the morning of November 16, this unequal exchange had claimed the life of a German doctor, Harry Fischer, who had ventured out on the street to help an injured neighbor and been struck by an Israeli shell. For two hours his badly mutilated body lay on the road. The German ambassador and representatives of the Red Cross were unable to get the Israelis to agree to a cease-fire so it could be retrieved.

In the morning, the international press visited the shrine erected on the spot where Dr. Fischer died. It briefly seemed that the death of a German doctor might put what UN Human Rights Commissioner Mary Robinson has called Israel's "excessive and disproportionate use of force" back on the front pages, reinforcing the urgent Palestinian call for international protection.

But by late November, with the number of Palestinians killed during two months of clashes reaching 260, a third of them children, news of the deaths had become routine in the two countries that could stop them - Israel and the USA. For the Israeli public "four Palestinians killed is an ordinary occurrence," wrote award-winning Israeli journalist Amira Haas. "Nobody asks if it is logical that an unarmed 15-year-old was hit in the head by the precise bullet of a trained sniper" (Ha'aretz, November 15).

Israeli sharpshooters using silencers target Palestinian demonstrators aged 12 and up. "So why kill, why not just injure?" Amira Haas asked an Israeli army sharpshooter in an interview published in the widely-read Ha'aretz (November 20). "If you decide to wound people, more people will get hurt, and the question is whether this is better. Wounding fans anger even more," was the response.

In early November the Physicians for Human Rights reported that 33 Palestinian ambulances had been hit by Israeli gunfire, with 17 completely destroyed. The New-York based Committee to Protect Journalists documented the cases of ten journalists wounded by Israeli bullets during the same period of time. Israelis I talked to who considered themselves part of the peace movement found it hard to come to terms with these facts.

For its part, the mainstream US media rarely questions the Israeli assertion that the use of high-powered weapons, attack helicopters, missiles, tanks, gunboats, house demolitions, "sweeps" that clear the ground of Palestinian-owned buildings and olive groves, blockades, and an economic siege against a largely unarmed population constitutes "restraint." Few Americans know why Palestinians have given up on what many in the US still assume was a genuine "peace process," and how deeply implicated this country is in its failure.

Had the US truly been an "honest broker" of the Oslo process, Israel would not have been able to use the last seven years to establish the sort of "facts on the ground" that virtually rule out the creation of a viable Palestinian state. For a brief moment in 1991, President Bush used Israel's request for $10 billion in loan guarantees to demand a settlement freeze. But the standoff was short-lived, and Israel eventually received practically all of the loan guarantees in addition to its $3 billion plus in annual US aid. Meanwhile, throughout the '90s it steadily confiscated Palestinian land, doubled the number of settlers to 370,000, and created a network of "Jewish only" by pass roads in the West Bank that turned areas of Palestinian autonomy into isolated islands.

Today, large numbers of US citizens are amassed on both sides of the barricades. American-born settlers, estimated at 15 percent of the total settler population in the early 1990s, have taken up residence throughout the West Bank and Gaza, claiming Palestinian territory as "theirs". Americans should not be proud of their settler exports. One of them, Rabbi Moshe Levinger, has for years made the lives of his Hebron neighbors miserable. He was lionized by his followers for his ten weeks spent in prison in 1990 for the unprovoked killing of a Palestinian merchant. Brooklyn-born Baruch Goldstein, who remains a hero to many religious settlers, gunned down thirty Palestinians during Ramadan prayers in Hebron's Ibrahim Mosque in February 1994. Six years earlier, during the first Palestinian intifada, Goldstein told Ellen Hosmer, editor-at-large for Multinational Monitor:

"This land belongs to us, and the Arabs don't belong to us, so the land we should keep. The Arabs we should let go. I think it's feasible to do so.
Militarily it's no problem...Will the world accept us driving them out? Eventually we're going to have to drive them out or we'll be driven out. Either one or the other."
Today, Israeli settlers are being "protected" by American-made armored jeeps, armaments, missiles and helicopters, purchased with my tax dollars and used against Palestinians, some of whom, like my formerly stateless Beit Jala host and thousands of other residents of the West Bank and Gaza Strip, hold US citizenship. The US, mute before Israel's repeated violations of international law governing the actions of an occupier, will only agree to a UN protection force if it is called for by Israel. Meanwhile, President Clinton in mid-November asked Congress for another $450 million in military aid to Israel.

How can the United States be moved to adopt a more "even-handed" policy in the region? I returned from the West Bank and Gaza Strip with two thoughts - one pure fantasy, the other fast becoming a reality.

As I entered the 22-mile-long prison that is the Gaza Strip, home to 1.2 million Palestinians caged in by an electrified fence and military checkpoints, I wished for the company of those US politicians, opinion-makers and censors who had over the decades done so much to silence and distort the Palestinian side of the story.

What would they think of the suffocating conditions of total closure, of the strangled economy, of the dwindling food, fuel and medical supplies, of ambulances carrying the seriously injured being held up indefinitely at checkpoints? What would they say to the boy in the "USA" T-shirt, who stood with his family in the ruins of their house in Rafah that had just been bulldozed by Israeli soldiers who still control the area between the Gaza Strip and Egypt, and spend their days firing live ammunition at children who hurl rocks into the no-man's land? How would they regard the few thousand settlers who control 30 percent of the Gaza Strip, have their own roads to travel on and keep their swimming pools full and sprinklers working while 700,000 Palestinians are crammed into refugee camps and drink water contaminated with salt. What would they make of our visit to Nasser Hospital in Khan Yunis, where we talked to children from the village of Deir al-Balah wounded in their homes by gunfire from the nearby heavily-fortified settlement of Kfar Darom? How would they react to the Kfar Darom settlers and soldiers who blocked the main north-south artery in the Gaza Strip with tanks and played soccer in the road as Palestinians fumed in their overheated cars and buses?

If those behind America's uncritical support of Israel are unlikely to put themselves in a position where they are obliged to face up to what seven years of Oslo have meant for Palestinians, the Qatar-based television station Al-Jazeera is making sure that the Arab "street" will be fully informed. The detailed coverage which this internationally-acclaimed CNN-style station is giving to the Israeli repression of the Al-Aqsa intifada is reaching into households around the region, inflaming sentiment against the United States. If regional stability is a prime concern for US policy-makers, they have plenty to worry about now.

So do the Israelis. Sooner or later they are going to have to face the facts and deal with the consequences of their "Greater Israel" land grab: they cannot have their settlements and peace too. As Israeli peace activist Uri Avnery wrote in his November 25th letter to the settlers:
"Every settlement is a land mine on the road to peace. You are sitting on the land reserves of the Palestinian people, on stolen land, and use the water needed by another people for their very existence. You dominate the landscape; symbols of the occupation...What you and the soldiers are doing now is creating such rage and hatred among the Palestinians, that your continued presence in the territories has become impossible. Every dead child, every demolished home, every 'blockade' and 'liquidation' brings the end of your settlements nearer."

It is not strictly necessary for US legislators to stand on a roof near Bethlehem to comprehend the truth of this message - but it might help.
