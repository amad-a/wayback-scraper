Thoughtless Pens..!
    By Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj

    I have an old friend who returned to his homeland full of passion and excitement after a long exile abroad.  Perhaps allowing some refugees to return home was the most important accomplishment of the Olso Agreement.

    This friend of mine had been living in London. Despite his luxurious life, his British Passport, and his many successful businesses, including a major publishing house, he always dreamt of returning to Palestine.  He adores our country and has always considered Gaza as his first home.

    My friend has told me many stories about life in exile since his departure from Gaza many years ago. One of the most interesting and meaningful stories that he told me was about his desire to publish an Arabic magazine in London.  After seriously considering this idea, he phoned the Ministry of the Interior to inquire about the possibility of obtaining a permit to publish the magazine.  The Ministry employee replied that his Ministry did not give permits for magazines and newspapers.

    My friend was at loss or that perhaps-even England was like the Arab, where to go in a place, not like the Arab World, that has no ministry of information, indeed miss-information.
     
    He decided to call the Ministry of Trade & Commerce, hoping to obtain a permit.  Due to the commercial nature of magazines, he thought, the Ministry must have a specialized department for publications.  Once again, however, he was told that the Ministry does not issue permits for magazines.

    Thoroughly confused and frustrated, my friend eventually consulted a lawyer on the issue.

    The lawyer was surprised by the request and told my friend, with a slight degree of humor, that there was no need for a permit and that anyone could publish anything that he wanted - books, magazines, or newspapers.

    My friend told me this story when I asked him to publish an Arabic periodical about democratic awareness.  He explained to me in diplomatic terms that we would need to obtain a permit!  We would have to present our periodical to a censor, in accordance with the law of publication which was gives passed by the PA prior to the election and the ministry of information much power and leverage.

    Few weeks ago, I have read a statement by the Palestinian Attorney General before his resignation in which he said that we are living in the shadow of the Emergency Laws issued by the British Mandatory power which has introduced the law in order to repress the Palestinian resistance.  I could not believe it.  These laws restrict freedom, forbid democracy, and destroy creativity and originality.  I then looked back to the Palestinian Declaration of Independence, where I found many passages emphasizing the rule of law and respect for human rights and freedom of expressions.  In addition, I examined the Basic Law, passed by the Legislative Council but still await the daylight.

    Unfortunately, many intellectuals and writers prefer to be safe, or they think so, by specializing in Israeli or Mao Mao affairs – issues that are comfortable and free from possible confrontation.

    The result is that in Palestine today we either have thoughts without writers or writers without thoughts.

    This is a clear shortcoming that we must address.  Accepting this situation through ‘nonalignment’ stands is a direct opposition to the principles of democracy.  The Legislative Council must take a bold step and cancel the Printing and Publications Law.  At the same time, free writers must defend their rights as expressed in the Palestinian Declaration of Independence and in all international declarations of basic human rights.
