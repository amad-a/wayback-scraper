“Putting the Palestinian House in Order”
 By Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj
 

 
As expected, the London peace conference failed to resuscitate the moribund peace process. Likewise, conferences in Cairo, Amman, Oslo, and Washington will fail. Things are crystal clear now, and talking about the inflexibility of the Israeli government is time wasting regurgitation; so is talking about American submission to Zionist pressures, the weak European role, and hope in a strong Arab stance. Netanyahu’s plan is to convince the world that his generous 9% pullback from bits and pieces of the West Bank is the most any Israeli leader can do, so that the world stops asking him for further withdrawals.

With the return home of the Palestinian delegation carrying yet more disappointment and despair, we must do what we have failed to do since signing the Oslo Accords: review the strategy of Palestinian politics. In this respect, the Palestinian Authority needs to confront serious questions, take a stance on a number of issues, solve the sense of alienation that has plagued Palestinians in Palestine and the Diaspora, and address why the world stands like a scarecrow in the face of Israeli actions.

How do we restore hope to the Palestinian people? How do we make the rest of the world shift from being a mere bystander to an active player?

My belief is firm in that change starts from within, from Palestine, from the relationship between the people and authority, and the respect for human rights, democracy, and the law. These issues have an immediate impact on the credibility of the Palestinian Authority inside and outside Palestine.

Palestinians in exile who contributed money and time to the Intifada are now crippled because Oslo and its aftereffects have marginalized their role. In addition, the reports of corruption and mismanagement in the PA, the violations of citizens’ rights, and the ineffectiveness of the Palestinian Legislative Council leave those Palestinians in a state of confusion in the least. In reality the majority of Diaspora Palestinians feel that their issue and cause have entered a dark maze with no guiding light or hope.

The feelings of confusion and frustration also torment the friends of the Palestinian people throughout the world, as one sees when traveling from place to place. These feelings mingle with the sense of powerlessness and paralysis, and everyone asks, “What are we to do?”

For them, the picture changed after Oslo, the establishment of the PA, the reports of disordered performance and chaotic decision-making, the possibility of internal fighting, and the PA’s disregard for the principles of democracy and human rights. Our friends ask us, “How are we to stand by you and raise our voices for you when you are such?”

Moreover, the enemies of the Palestinian people have increased their campaign to discredit the Palestinians and showcase them as a people incapable of ruling themselves; and thus, justifying the return of the occupation and its practices.

This makes it mandatory for us to reconsider our stance and come up with new ways of dealing with ourselves, Israel, and the rest of the world.

We saw some pro peace activists demonstrating outside 10 Downing Street in London at the time of the London conference; their number did not exceed thirty people. This number does not correspond with the severity of the situation, the number of British friends of the Palestinian people, or the morality of our cause. How would things be if thousands of people demonstrated in London, Washington, European capitals, and even Israel? What if these people marched the streets of their respective cities, raising peace banners and calling for justice in Palestine?

If this were to happen, a popular movement world-wide would arise and have an immense impact on political decision making. It would be the best answer to the hollow rigidity and racism of the Israeli Government. However, change will not and cannot begin except from within Palestine with the people fully backing the Palestinian Authority, which in turn respects human rights, democracy, and the law, and puts forth an example of justice, equality, and credibility. Then the Palestinian house will be in order, deriving its strength from all Palestinians, supporters and opponents of the PA, all respecting the law. Only then will Palestinians in exile regain their vigor and renew their commitment.

Indeed, the world will change when they see us as a peaceful people struggling for our freedom and rights against racism.
