“If I were a Minister!”
    By Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj
    I was impressed by Abu Ali Shahien’s stance in face of the bullying tactics exhibited by settlers. And Shahien was not the only one to stand up to Israeli aggression, the world saw the Minister of Higher Education, Hanan Ashrawi, and Head of the Orient House, Faisal El-Husseini lead protests. We saw Israeli soldiers beat up Finance Minister, Zuhdi El-Nashashibi, despite his old age.
     
    These are clear signs indicating Ministers’ identification with the people’s struggle for freedom. These Ministers, and many of their colleagues, have a long history of resisting Israeli injustice and aggression.
     
    The Palestinian individual, whether a regular citizen or a Minister, must fight two battles simultaneously: the battle for freedom and regaining the land, and the battle to rebuild the homeland. In addition, ministers are evaluated by their performance in their respective ministries, and we know of many who work hard and dedicate much time and effort to developing their society and to serving citizens to the best of their ability.
     
    An example is the Ministry of Education. Despite the poor conduct in dealing with striking teachers, the Ministry has employed specialized professionals to upgrade and improve the Ministry; and we are anxiously waiting to see how these professionals will implement the curriculum design plan of Dr. Ibrahim Abu El-Lughod.
     
    Other such examples include the Ministry of Higher Education, the Ministry of Commerce, and the Ministry of Housing. And these examples should not be taken as a detraction from the efforts of other ministers who are working very hard, despite all of the talk about the Cabinet reshuffle (and despite having their resignation accepted by the President!).
     
    However, regardless of the reshuffle and all that the ministries accomplish, ministers remain a topic of gossip and jokes. There is public outcry over the deterioration in services, increasing bureaucracy, low salaries, conflicting tasks and responsibilities, internal power struggles, lack of planning, ministers who spend long tours abroad, random employment without qualifications and nepotism. These issues are what thousands of patients, government workers, teachers, engineers, physicians, soldiers, and civil workers are complaining about. How can this be when we have some excellent examples of credible ministers?
     
    In my opinion, the problem is not that of a minister or a ministry, and it will not be solved if the cabinet is reshuffled once or ten times. The dilemma is that the Authority lacks a system respected by all, a system that respects the people and adheres to the Law, one that ensures equality and justice.
     
    The ministers suffer from insufficient transparency and openness in the system; and the Cabinet lacks a sense of teamwork in carrying out the responsibilities of the Authority. The Cabinet is not a ticket to sit in elaborate meetings, ride around in luxury cars, and breeze through checkpoints with VIP passes which, incidentally, are not always respected -- just like ministerial decisions.
     
    It is not only the Cabinet that is plagued with inefficiency. The Palestinian court system is constantly humiliated for not implementing court rulings; and the Palestinian Legislative Council still regurgitates hollow promises made by the executive branch. The Council now is a hopeless structure.
     
    The principal issue at hand is the violation of the sanctity of the individual and the institution, through the lack of an order based on the separation of powers, democracy, and respect of the Law. This problem endangers all national institutions, and simply changing some ministers will not solve it.
     
    If I were a minister, I would ask myself where the Cabinet stands on the issues of breaching high court decisions, of human rights violations by security branches, of fighting corruption, of resolving the administrative chaos, and other pressing problems. These questions require the ministers to take a stance similar to the one that Shahien took in face Israeli aggression. In essence, it is all one battle that starts from within.
