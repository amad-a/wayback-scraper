“When will the Arab Regime fall?”
    By Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj

    Airports symbolize travel and invoke nostalgic feelings and longing for family and friends.  They also represent renewal and exploring the unknown.  I’m still amazed at an airplane’s ability to fly and how mankind has broken the secrets of avionics to dominate the world.

    Finally the plane landed in Berlin, the rejuvenated German capital recovering its glory after years of being a divided city.  The Berlin wall was a harsh symbol that makes one wonder how it ever fallen and disappeared.  How did the German masses from both sides of the wall finally met with the whole world watching and listening to the songs of peace and freedom?  At the time, the world had a lot of hope for freedom.

    I pictured Gorbachev as I walked Berlin’s main street where the famous Brandenberg Gate between East and West Berlin stands.  He lit the first candle that illuminated Moscow, signaling the downfall of barriers and end of the old decadent regimes.

    I remember how the Arab world was amazed as they witnessed Chauchesco topple down from his throne after decades of iron fist rule in Romania.  In his last few days, he vainly tried to escape the wrath of the people who drank from his bitter cup of oppression.  This was the course and end of the East European, revolutionary communist regimes.  These Leninist, Stalinist revolutionaries grabbed power and forced a one-party system, while ruthlessly eradicating opposition, for the greater good of the people!

    For the world to be a better place, communists grabbed power at whatever cost under the auspices of a Proletariat rule and considered opposition to communism enemies of the revolution that must be removed.  The result was torture, murder, imprisonment, and uprooting in the name of the people and protecting the revolution.

    Lenin’s philosophy of oppression was the first nail in the coffin of Communism and Marxism, which analyzed social structure and conflicts and called for eliminating all forms of oppression and build a democratic socialist society.

    Unfortunately, Leninist thought spread quickly in developing countries suffering from the effects of colonialism, ignorance, and poverty.  In such countries, those who called themselves revolutionaries believed that they were the elite leadership for their people.  They had no doubt that they have a historic responsibility to take the shortest route to power, by coup or armed struggle.

    Thus, revolutions and coups engulfed the Arab world, Africa, and Latin America in the name of freeing the people.  Governments that squander resources and enslave the people ruled many countries.  Power was in the hands of officers and uncrowned kings not better than colonialists or their puppet regimes in thinking that the land and people are their private possessions.  They degraded the people, spread corruption through their perverse governing, and forced the mind and will of the people in submission.  These regimes were outstanding in using the slogans of liberating Palestine and the struggle against colonialism as a justification to continue their autocracy.

    As I walked the Berlin’s main street, I could not help but ask myself, “how strange is it for dictatorships to tumble under popular democratic uprisings in many parts of the world, while the regimes stand defiant and strong in our Arab world?”

    It is clear that the peaceful, unarmed, and yet determined masses in the streets requesting their right to human dignity and to participate in the decision making process is the way to bring down these regimes.  This is why Iran’s Shah ran away, Chauchesco tried to run away before getting killed, Marcos fled, and the Berlin wall went down.  It will also be the way Soharto is brought down in Indonesia.

    It seems that the deep belief in the role of the people has not infected the Arab world.  The exception to this is the Palestinian Intifada in its first few years when it was a spontaneous popular uprising against the Israeli occupation.  The Intifada remains as an example of how people can go out in the streets and demand their rights.  There was trace of an intifada in Egypt when workers took to the streets demand their rights inciting Sadat to call it “the uprising of the thieves”.  Unfortunately, the majority of the Arab world lives hostage to the police state that succeeded in oppressing opposition or forcing it in submission while spreading a atmosphere of fear and uncertainty.

    Will the day come for the Arab masses to go out in the streets and start an Intifada against the decadent regimes, forcing their symbols to flee or commit suicide?  Will Lenin finally die in our Arab world?

    I know that this day is not distant.
