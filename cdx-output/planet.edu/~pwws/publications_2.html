Post Bejjing: Report on the Conditions of Women Working in Manufactures in the West Bank

Introduction

Since its establishment in 1981, the Palestinian Working Women Society has adopted the issue of working women's legal rights. The Society has contributed to the development of a policy defending working women's rights, as well as to increasing women's participation in the economic, political and social sectors. This was based on an integrated approach to development emphasizing empowerment as a key principle. In the early eighties, the focus was on improving the conditions of Palestinian women working in Israeli factories, in local workshops in the fields of pharmaceutical, textile, food industries, as well as in the service industries such as secretarial, nursing and education, in addition to other informal sectors.

During this period, the objectives were to educate Palestinian women about their legal rights, to bring them together in the Trade Union, to sign collective contracts in order to improve their working conditions in terms of contracts, specific working hours, annual and weekly vacations and wage increases. By the nineties, the Society's work expanded to include broader issues such as: promoting women's leadership and decision-making skills, participating in labor union policies, influencing public and governmental institutions regarding workers' training policy as well as the issue of a Palestinian labor law for women.

The Palestinian Working Women Society strives to achieve the above objectives by raising women's awareness about their rights, encouraging women to express themselves, studying arid analyzing the Palestinian labor law, organizing several social and educational activities, in addition to a networking through training, workshops in women right, cooperation with local, regional and international labor development institutions.

With the present study, the Society aims to inspire human rights and women's institutions, and the Ministry of Labour, to continue their efforts in improving women's economic conditions - particularly those working in workshops - as well as addressing inequalities between men and women.

In these manufactures, there are 25,230 workers - which represents 28.6% of the total number of workers in the industrial, commercial and service sectors. GOAL

The goal of this research is to highlight the importance of the manufacture sector in our society and to demonstrate the harsh conditions in which they live. It is hoped that the different ministries, NGO's trade unions, human rights institutions and women's centers defending women's rights will pay more attention to this neglected sector, which has a direct impact on the Gross National Product (GNP). This research will act as a primary reference for all social and economic development institutions striving to design and implement programs to improve the working conditions in the manufacture sector.

METHODOLOGY

This research is based on a random sample of 100 questionnaires filled out by women working in the manufacture sector. This represents 1. 74% of all women working in this sector. The questionnaires covered areas of the West Bank according to the geographical distribution, as shown in Table 2. The majority of this research focused on women working in the clothing industry. The clothing industry has the highest percentage (78%) of women workers because Palestinian manufactures are often sub-contracted by Israeli manufactures in this industry. The research also uses official statistics issued by the Palestinian Bureau of Statistics and on other data obtained from the UNDP Human Development Report in Palestine (1996-97) to weigh against this research.

This report addresses three main issues:

  • social, educational and health conditions
  • working conditions
  • income levels anti poverty
1. Social, Educational and Health Conditions

The average age of women working in manufactures is 26.9 years old, showing the youth of women workers. It is also the average age of the Palestinian people as a whole.

The average number of persons in these women's families 7.55 persons. This result is higher than the average for the West Bank (6.7 persons per family) and for Palestine (7.06 persons) in 1996. 48% of female worker live in villages, 22% live in camps and 30% in cities. This means that 70% are living in remote and primitive areas of the West Bank. However, the overall picture becomes clearer when one looks at educational levels. 50% of the women living in primitive areas have finished high school, 0.5% graduated from college and 0.05% completed university. The research also demonstrates that all of them (100%) can read and write. These results show that women workers are educated - even more than the percentage of educated women in Palestine. Unfortunately, they were prevented from continuing their education due to social and economic problems, etc.

Soscial Status:

79% are single and 15% are married. Others (6%) are either widows or divorced.

The data indicates that those who are married began working because they are the main family support. The percentage of married women who are only bread-winner for their families is 1401c, which is double the percentage in the West Bank (7.8%) and in Palestine (7.3%).

Services and Living Conditions:

Results show that 80% of the women workers' families own the houses in which they live . This is slightly less than the West Bank average in 1995 which was 81.8% - thus reflecting lower standards of living.

The fact that families own their house in the countryside does not necessarily mean that their living standards are high. Houses in villages are often very old, difficult to live in, lack services and are in need of restoration. 2. Working Conditions

According to this research, most of the women working in the clothing, workshops spread in all around of Palestine as a result of dealing with Israeli factories. Most women work for a daily wage that prevent them of enjoying rights such as holidays, vacations, insurance, savings, raises, etc. The average age monthly wage on a daily basis is 192 US$. The average monthly wage on a monthly basis is 216 US$. Therefore, the overall monthly wage is 204 US$. According to the PBS such a wage Is less than the estimated monthly wage in Palestine, which is about 392 US$.

Despite the low wages, 51% of female workers pay for their transportation and this is indeed a heavy burden for them especially given that 48% them live in villages and work in cities.

Results:

  • 79% of female workers do not have annual vacations nor sick leave; only 13% have annual vacations and 8% have both;
  • 0% have maternity leave;
  • 24% of them have a health insurance (most of them are under the governmental insurance);
  • 38% of those insured are covered by their employers for half of the costs; (in other words, 9% possess an insurance that pays half the costs).

3. Women workers and Poverty

Speculations regarding poverty in Palestine differ according to the variety of such studies. The poverty line is between 550 US$ and 904 US$ per person per year. Experts agree that 855 US$ is the average minimum family income sufficient to meet the needs of families in Palestine.

The Palestinian Bureau of Statistics estimates that families who spend more than 50% of their income for food can be classified as "low-income" level, representing 38.5% of the Palestinian population. In an attempt to have a overview of poverty averages in the families of women working in manufactures, it has been decided to add the average income of an individual to their families.

As a result, we found that 32.2% of the families have an individual with an income of maximum $855 per year, while the percentage in Palestine as a whole is 25% of the population.

Results show that 24% of the families pay more than 50% of their income for food in comparison with 38.5% in Palestine. We can consider this average as an indicator of poverty among this sector of Palestinian workers. And we can suggest that 28% of the families live below the poverty line.

The Palestinian Working Women Society (PWWS) is a non-governmental grassroots organization located in Ramallah, West Bank. The PWWS was founded on March 8, 1981 as the "Union of Palestinian Working Women". Since its creation, the PWWS has been continuously active in women's affairs and in the Palestinian national struggle against the Israeli occupation. The PWWS runs several activities reflecting our vision which is translated into four main programs:

  • Counseling Program
  • Working Women Program
  • Lobbying and Networking activities
  • Promoting Democracy and Civic Education

Our Vision:

To empower and strengthen the participation of Palestinian women both in the private and public lives in all regions through the integration of women in building the Palestinian civil society.

Our Goals:

The PWWS strives to:

  • promote the Palestinian women's participation in the democratization process towards a society in which women will enjoy equal rights with men together with social justice, equality and opportunities in all respects.
  • educate Palestinian women about their constitutional rights and to encourage public support for women's rights.
  • provide social support services for women through the counseling program.
  • influence public and governmental policies in order to eliminate all kinds of discrimination against women.
