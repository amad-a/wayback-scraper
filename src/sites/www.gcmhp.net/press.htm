PRESS RELEASE
GAZA COMMUNITY MENTAL HEALTH PROGRAMME
THIRD INTERNATIONAL CONFERENCE
OCTOBER 13-15, 1997
HEALTH AND HUMAN RIGHTS
DAY 1 - OCTOBER 13

The Gaza Community Mental Health Programme's third international conference began today in the Rashad El-Shawa Cultural Center in Gaza City, Palestine. The theme of this year's October 13-15 conference is Health and Human Rights. The conference brings together over 400 health professionals, scholars, and activists from throughout the world to discuss the still-grim situation faced by Palestinians in the Occupied Territories and the Palestinian semi-autonomous territory.

Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj, Director of the Gaza Community Mental Health Programme (GCMHP) and Commissioner General of the Palestinian Independent Commission for Citizens' Rights delivered the Opening Speech. Dr. El-Sarraj raised the concern that ongoing political tensions led the Israeli government to reject travel permits for some invited West Bank residents and one American citizen holding an Israeli identity card (please see forthcoming press release).

During his speech, Dr. El-Sarraj referred to the nascent experience of Palestinian democracy and the ongoing struggle in securing respect for Palestinian human rights. He emphasized the need for Palestinians to press ahead with building democracy and strengthening dialogue with the Palestinian Authority (PA). In concluding, Dr. El-Sarraj expressed his gratitude and appreciation for all those who supported him in 1996 when he was arrested by the PA. He described the experience as painful, but as opening the road to a more meaningful dialogue with the PA which has led to advancements in the human rights situation.

Dr. Kamal El-Shrafi, head of the Monitoring and Ethics Committee of the Palestinian Legislative Council (PLC) raised the question of how Palestinians can move from backwardness and subordination and toward pluralism and democracy. He expressed his concern over the internal and external pressures recently exerted on the PA to take certain measures thought to advance peace and development. According to El-Shrafi, such pressures have resulted in the PA resorting to arbitrary arrests and severe interrogation methods which have led to death. El-Shrafi insisted that the PA, while not a fully sovereign state, must abide by international standards of human rights.

El-Shrafi detailed the close relationship between health and human rights and community development and poverty alleviation. Poverty exerts a destructive impact on health and human rights which cannot be ignored. With 70 percent of the Palestinian population living in poverty, Dr. El-Shrafi asserted that it is imperative to meet basic human needs through employment, housing, and personal security. Furthermore, freedom, human rights, and democracy are vital not just for the community, but as a base for the development process itself. The national government ought to show a strong commitment to democratic institutions, protect human rights and active political participation of the people, and ascertain the rule of law, transparency, and accountability.

El-Shrafi further declared the importance of women‘s rights. Palestinian society must move forward on two legs, with equal rights for women, or risk becoming unbalanced. The needs of Palestinian children must also be given prime attention because of the difficult conditions in which they grew up during the Intifada. Such children need support in their rehabilitation efforts.

Dr. El-Shrafi forcefully condemned Israeli occupation policies which have systematically violated Palestinian human rights on an individual and collective level. International condemnation has been impeded by U.S. vetoes. Despite the Madrid conference and the subsequent agreements, Israel continues to violate Palestinian human rights. Israeli torture of Palestinians has resulted in deaths and the Israeli High Court of Justice has gone far in condoning startling violations of basic rights.

The Plenary Session followed the remarks of Dr. El-Sarraj and Dr. El-Shrafi.

Dr. Jonathan Fine, Former Executive Director of Physicians for Human Rights spoke on The Unfinished Task: Global Campaigning for Health and Human Rights. Dr. Fine asserted that over 1&frac12; billion people are suffering from degrading poverty throughout the world and that some of them could be found only one or two kilometers from the conference site. There is an arrogant policy of governments around the world to neglect the poor. Genocides rage uncontrolled throughout the world and the Vatican speaks of an ice-cold indifference to the unprecedented abyss of death in Algeria where approximately 100,000 deaths have occurred in the past five years. Despite the many problems throughout the world, Dr. Fine asserted that there are things that health professionals and conference participants can do to make an effective difference.

First, health professionals and conference participants should support every national health professional group committed to advancing human rights. Second, participants must demonstrate unity and strength in asserting the universality of their deepest beliefs, while maintaining the importance of individual identities and cultures. Third, participants must let the world community know what their vision is. Fourth, participants must consider what form the effort for human rights will take. Such a world movement can advance health professionals everywhere and put an end to economic exploitation and discrimination.

The Personal Representative of President Yasser Arafat, Dr. Suliman Shourafa spoke briefly following Dr. Fine. He was introduced by Dr. El-Sarraj, who asserted that the dialogue must never be abandoned, but that human rights advocates will also not retreat or succumb. Dr. Shourafa asserted that the President would make every effort to help the conference succeed, and indeed, this is the most successful international conference held by the GCMHP to date.

Dr. James Welsh, President of the Medical Department of Amnesty International spoke on Doctors Campaigning Against Human Rights Violations. Dr. Welsh declared that there are four areas in which medical professionals can help advance human rights. These are:

    1. Access to prisoners and those at risk .
    2. Use of medical skills to determine whether there are traces of torture.
    3. Influence. People listen to what health professionals have to say.
    4. Ethics. Health professionals conduct themselves by certain values.
Dr. Welsh also outlined four areas which could be strengthened. These are:
    1. To protest constructively. Letters of protest can be better channeled.
    2. To create greater awareness of human right and instill values.
    3. To render practical assistance in working with victims.
    4. To educate others more fully on crucial human rights issues.
Dr. Welsh concluded by stating that Palestinian health professionals now have a chance to imbed ethical health practices in the health system of the PA.
Dr. Nancy Murray of the Middle East Justice Network and the Bill of Rights Education Project finished the Plenary Session with her presentation on The United States, Human Rights, and the Myth of the ‘Honest Broker.‘ Dr. Murray critiqued Secretary of State Madeleine Albright’s description of Palestinian and Israeli leaders as trapeze artists jumping towards each other with the U.S. serving as the safety net. She asserted that the safety net would probably not work as effectively for Palestinian leaders plunging to their doom as it would for Israeli leaders. She asserted that while the Jewish lobby, particularly AIPAC, has strength in the United States, the real reasons for U.S. policies result from geo-political considerations in which the U.S. seeks to control the flow of resources and the selling of arms. She also expressed concern over the role of U.S. evangelicals in the development of U.S. policy to the region.
Dr. Murray expressed the sentiment that Palestinian leaders would have done better in the U.S. had they reached into the U.S. grassroots during the Intifada along the lines of what the African National Congress did at a similar historicaljuncture. Instthe Palestinian leadership has suffered under a succof policy retreats by the U.S. government. She expressed concern that the U.S. now sees itself as managing a process in which everything is linked to Israeli security requirements and in keeping the talks going rather than in working toward the fulfillment of agreements. In conclusion she did express some hope that Europe would begin to be more assertive in pushing for a role in the peace process.
Three concurrent workshop sessions were held in the morning. In the first one on Perspectives on Human Rights, Ms. Laura Drake of Solidarity International for Human Rights spoke on The Enclosure of Palestine: The Oslo Process as Seen from Washington and Gaza. She outlined former Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin‘s tactic of separation as one meant to preserve Israeli democracy by avoiding the absorption of 2 million Palestinians. Rabin‘s separation policy created three Palestinian cantons with a fourth canton in Jericho. With separation Israel found a way to draw away from the Palestinian people without drawing away from the land. Oslo II has had the effect of enclosing and dissecting Palestinian territory. In conclusion, she asserted that Palestinians are not free so long as Israel controls the borders and so long as Israeli security concerns are placed on a higher ethical plane than Palestinian freedom and rights.

Mr. Isam Younis of the Palestinian Center for Human Rights spoke on Social and Economic Rights in Gaza and the West Bank: Rights and Responsibilities. He made three main points: 1) During the Intifada civil and political rights were addressed and not social and economic rights. 2) The PA has the responsibility of respecting and monitoring human rights. 3) Israel has flagrantly violated the Geneva Conventions.

Mr. Wajeeh Yaghi of the Palestinian Legislative Council spoke on Human Rights: Islam and the 1948 UN General Assembly International Declaration of Human Rights. Mr. Yaghi declared that contrary to what outsiders say, Islam gives full rights to women and that oppression does not signify a defect in Islam but in the individual person. The conception disseminated in the West that women are forced into submission is unacceptable. In fact, Islam can accommodate the entire scope of human rights as defined by the West.

Professor Augustus Richard Norton of Boston University spoke on Political Inclusion and the Discourse of Dissent in the Middle East. He argued that political inclusion is integral to a democratic society. To allow the opposition to operate, he said, tends to promote a vibrant political system.

Mr. Sa’ad Hamid of The Center for Economic and Social Rights delivered a presentation on Developing a Common Framework for the Promotion of Economic, Social, and Cultural Rights. He asserted that social and economic rights are put on a lower ethical plane than civil and political rights. He added that there should be clearly defined limits and responsibilities for all parties involved in the peace process.

The second workshop was on Women‘s Rights in Palestine. Ms. Abeer Jabir, Ms. Azza Muheisin, and Ms. Na’la Afana of the GCMHP spoke on Women Empowerment Project: Achievements and Obstacles. The speakers expressed concern over a variety of problems besetting Palestinian women, including poverty, early marriage, divorce, domestic violence, and insufficient education.

Dr. Abdallah Abu Eid of Al-Najah University spoke on Rights of Palestinian Women: An Analysis of Some Palestinian Draft Laws. Dr. Abu Eid spoke about some weaknesses in the Basic Law project and the failure to use the words he and she. He also raised concern over the protection of the rights of children.

Ms. Nahda Younis Shihada of the Ministry of Planning and International Cooperation spoke on Working Women‘s Rights: Reality and Expectation. Ms. Younis Shihada detailed the importance of equal work opportunities for women, safe work environments for women, and the importance of work done by women without financial remuneration.

Mr. Majdi Baddah of the Association of Islamic Sciences Graduates delivered a presentation on Women‘s Rights in Islam in which he asserted that Islam distinguishes between men and women because there are physical and physiological differences between them. The roles of men and women differ as a result.

The morning‘s third workshop was on Children‘s Rights. Dr. Naama Havkin of Tel Aviv University presented a paper on Injured Children Become (more often than not) Injuring Parents. (Humiliation of Children Increases the Masses of Humiliating, Spiteful, Restless, Unfortunates, and Sick in our Society.) Dr. Havkin outlined the history of abuse against children and expressed the fear that children who are oppressed will become, in turn, parents who oppress their own children.

Dr. Charlotte Cole of the Children‘s Television Workshop spoke on Developing an Educational Curriculum for Rechov Sumsum/Shara’a Simsim, a Preschool Television Series Designed to Teach Mutual Respect. She declared that children can benefit from television programs and that such programs can complement the teaching process and provide children with a sense of culture and difference.

Ms. Nadia Taysir Dabbagh of University College London delivered a presentation on Children‘s Rights: A Medical-Anthropological Perspective. She declared that children have legal and moral rights. She provided an example of how the media affect our conceptions of reality by analyzing how an Israeli child might be seen as a victim and a Palestinian child as an activist (read: aggressor).

In the afternoon, six workshops were held. The first session was on Medical Ethics: The Case of Israel. Dr. Derek Summerfield of the Medical Foundation for the Care of Victims of Torture set the framework for discussion with his presentation on Medical Ethics: The Case of the Israeli Medical Association. He argued first that in Israel torture has become a state policy. The policy is so bureaucratized that doctors approach torture in a routine manner. In such a state medical workers are forced to choose between the role of health worker and citizen. Israel has chosen a version of citizenship in which security concerns override medical responsibility. Secondly, Summerfield argued that Israel has violated medical neutrality in restricting the movement of medical workers and stalling the sick and dying at checkpoints. Dr. Ruchama Marton of Physicians for Human Rights in her speech on The Israeli Weltanschaung and Government Policies – The Duty of Intervention and Dr. Kamal El-Shrafi in his presentation on Medical Ethics: The Case of Khalid Abu Da’ya added their voices to Summerfield‘s arguments. Marton said that the idea of total separation, which has won support among the Israeli policy-makers, has only succeeded in retaining a hold on Palestinian land while dehumanizing the Palestinian people. El-Shrafi added an emotional force to the indictments. He showed a film in which medical workers examine the body of Khalid Abu Da’ya and display his very visible marks of torture. In the questioning session, Rami Heilbronn of the Medical Foundation for the Care of Victims of Torture, UK, said that the absence of Israeli Medical Association head Eran Dolev must be made into a presence. Dolev canceled his appearance at the conference only one day before it began, citing “very serious pressure.” Quoting Albert Einstein and Bertrand Russell, Heilbronn said,”Remember your humanity and forget the rest”. He then said, “Eran Dolev cannot forget the rest”. Summerfield then made a motion to draft a letter addressed to Dolev, in which he would put all the questions posed in the workshop to Dolev directly.

Mr. Sabby Sagall of the Socialist Review presented a paper on From ‘The Chosen People‘ to the Holocaust: A Marxist Psychoanalytical Approach to Zionism. Mr. Sagall claimed that Zionism is the product of the final decaying phase of capitalism and that the Zionist project tried to jumpstart nationalism when nationalism was out of date. They could only achieve this through British and then American imperialism.

The seworkshop was on Rehabilitating Survivors of Human Rights Viola. Dr. Maria Piniou-Kalli of the IRCT Bureaand the Medical Rehabilitation Center for Torture Victims - Athens, spoke on The Regionalization of Activities Concerning Human Rights. Dr. Piniou-Kalli asserted that despite major progress on issues of racism and discrimination there are still significant violations of human rights. A network of organizations has been established in the Mediterranean and Middle East regions to defend human rights. This network must lay the groundwork to rebuild proper human rights standards

Dr. Amar Jesani of the Research Center for Anusandhan Trust (CEHAT) spoke on The Medical Profession and Human Rights in India. Dr. Jesani declared that the situation of the medical profession at this time in India is full of violations of human rights. Furthermore, basic standards of medical care in India are not upheld and doctors occasionally help the police by providing false medical reports.

Dr. Dimocritos Sarantidis of the Medical Rehabilitation Center for Torture Victims spoke on Operation and Structure of MRCT, Athens: Intake Procedure, Registration, and Data of Clients. He detailed how his organization collects data on torture and the countries in which violations of human rights occur.

Dr. Daniel L. Creson of The University of Texas at Houston spoke on Psychosocial Rehabilitation Programs in Sarajevo during the City Siege: Implications for Post-War Activities. Dr. Creson declared that the psychosocial stresses in Sarajevo are many and include economic hardships, social disruption, physical/psychological violence, ethnic persecution, and the loss of homes.

The third afternoon workshop was on Torture and was to be chaired by Dr. Mustafa El-Barghouthi, but he was prevented from traveling to Gaza by the Israeli government (see other forthcoming press release). Ms. Sahar Francis of Al Dameer Prisoners Support Association spoke on Legitimizing Torture. Ms. Francis described the different forms of torture employed by the Israelis against the Palestinian population.

Dr. Diane Perlman delivered a speech on The Psychoanatomy of Political Terrorism: Unconscious Dynamics of Victimization-Perpetration, the Collusion of Bystanders, and Moral Responsibility. Dr. Perlman asserted that suffering, violence, injustice, and poverty will lead in the end to depression and frustration.

Dr. Beatrice M. Patsalides of Survivors International - San Francisco spoke on The Trauma of Torture: Psychodynamic Considerations of Transference and Countertransference. She declared that mental therapy is crucial for prisoners who have endured traumatic experiences.

 The fourth afternoon workshop was on Human Rights in Palestine. Mr. Khader Shkirat of the Palestinian Society for the Protection of Human Rights and the Environment spoke on Human Rights Violations under the Palestinian Authority. In arguing that the PA has observed an explicit and clear policy to disregard the guarantees and assurances of international law Shkirat sounded a note echoed and re-echoed by Mr. Raji Sourani and Dr. El-Sarraj. Each catalogued a long list of human rights violations perpetrated by the PA, then struggled with how to maintain loyalty to the major representative institution of the Palestinian people while remaining true to the dream of a civil and democratic society. In his talk, Mental Health and Human Rights Challenges in Palestine, Dr. El-Sarraj struggled with how the dream of a free and independent state had devolved into a state with authoritarian tendencies. As Mr. Sourani said in his presentation on Palestinian Human Rights: The Reality and Challenge, people have nothing to lose, and only become perplexed at the discrepancy between the political rhetoric and the daily reality. All three, however, cited the continuing Israeli occupation as a major obstacle to Palestinian democracy. Yet despite the deteriorating conditions all three affirmed their commitment to a civil and democratic society, even if that commitment means criticizing the PA.

The fifth afternoon workshop was on Forensic Psychiatry. Dr. Nimr Abu Zarka of the GCMHP spoke on Forensic Psychiatry in Palestine. Dr. Abu Zarka suggested the implementation of three recommendations. 1) Specialized psychiatrists should be sent outside of Gaza to gain more knowledge of Forensic Psychiatry. 2) Institutions should be established for mental health patients who have committed crimes so that they are not imprisoned with the general prison population. 3) Specialized laws should be established to deal with those patients who commit crimes.

Dr. Paul Nasser of Anderson & Nasser Clinical and Forensic Psychiatry delivered a presentation on Clinical and Forensic Psychiatry. He asserted the importance of patients providing clear information to their therapists about the exact details of their traumatic experiences.

The sixth afternoon workshop was on Rehabilitating Survivors of Human Rights Violations. Dr. Rony Berger of Al Quds University and The Hebrew University spoke on Healing the Body and Mind: A Somato-Emotional Approach to the Rehabilitation of Human Violation Victims. He asserted that trauma will make the body physically weak and ill. Activities that increase the victims‘ level of energy are crucial.

Dr. Federico Allodi of The Toronto Hospital spoke on Health and Human Rights Violations: A Model for Case Assessment and Report. Dr. Allodi asserted that it is critical to use culturally appropriate methods in treating victims of torture.
