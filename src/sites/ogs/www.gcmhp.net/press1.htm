PRESS RELEASE
GAZA COMMUNITY MENTAL HEALTH PROGRAMME
THIRD INTERNATIONAL CONFERENCE
OCTOBER 13-15,1997
HEALTH AND HUMAN RIGHTS
DAY 2 - OCTOBER 14

The Gaza Community Mental Health Programme's third international conference held its second day of activities today in the Rashad El-Shawa Cultural Center in Gaza City, Palestine. The theme of this year's October 13-15 conference is Health and Human Rights. The conference brings together over 400 health professionals scholars, and social activists from throughout the world to discuss the still-grim situation faced by Palestinians in the occupied territories and Palestinian semi-autonomous territory. The morning Plenary Session was marred by the absence of  Dr. Mustafa El-Barghouthi of the Union of Palestinian Medical Relief Committees. He was prevented by the Israeli authorities from traveling from his office in Beit Hanina to the conference in Gaza (see press release). Such absences are beginning to harm the integrity of an otherwise very successful conference. The Gaza Community Mental Health Programme (GCMHP) and conference participants condemn the intransigence of Israeli authorities involved in this matter. Freedom of travel is a basic human right that is being withdrawn by the Israeli authorities on a variety of pretexts.

In the Plenary Session, Professor Bent Sorensen, Vice-Chairman of the UN Committee against Torture, presented a paper on The UN Convention against Torture and the Case of Israel. In a scathing indictment of the ongoing Israeli policy of torture, Professor Sorensen detailed Israeli violations of the UN Convention against Torture. The Convention, according to Sorensen, clearly states that torture is not allowed; there can be no excuse for it whatsoever, and the act is not excusable even if one is under orders. Sorensen denounced the January 1994 Landau Commission report, which allowed the use of moderate physical pressure against detainees. He declared that the UN Committee against Torture considers the Landau Commission reports to be completely unacceptable and recommends that the secret torture procedures it outlines be published in full. Sorensen stated that the Committee expected to find torture in Israel during its investigations in 1994 and had proved its concerns by 1997. One clear example of torture in Israel is severe shaking. The Israelis mislabel this as the use of moderate physical pressure. Sorensen expressed his disappointment that Israel has recently stated that it will not be ready for a November meeting to discuss torture and the necessity of releasing the Landau Commission report in full.

Dr. Adriaan Van Es of the Johannes Wier Foundation delivered a speech entitled UN Special Rapporteurs on Health Professionals and Human Rights. In introductory remarks, Van Es gave his full support to the newly registered Physicians for Human Rights branch in East Jerusalem. He then said that, because of the unique access they have during times of political conflict or tension, health professionals are sometimes the only ones with detailed knowledge of human rights violations. This access necessitates proper reporting mechanisms. Dr. Van Es expressed the concern that the duty to report human rights violations often conflicts with the desires of violators to suppress such information. Health professionals risk a great deal in reporting such violations, but there is a real need to be wary of professional apathy and self-censorship. As a result, health professionals need legal protection and strong organizational backing. A UN rapporteur could help meet the need of health professionals for protection, but such a position should not be allowed to overlap with already existing UN jobs. Rather, the position should have integrative capacities. This rapporteur would receive, evaluate, investigate, and report allegations of threats directed at health professionals.

Dr. Rami Heilbronn of the Medical Foundation for the Care of Victims of Torture spoke powerfully and movingly during a speech entitled Bystanding and Whistle-blowing. He began his presentation by condemning the Israelis for not permitting the attendance of Dr. Barghouthi and by criticizing the Palestinian Authority for its continuing incarceration of Fathi Suboh (whose wife was present at today's conference). He then moved from the general to the specific in discussing the fragmentation of Jewish identity over the past 100 years. Israel, he declared, is an ethnocentric Jewish national state established to solve the persecution of Jews in Europe and cultural problems pertaining to secular Jewish identity. It solved neither; and in fact, according to Heilbronn, Israel is now the most dangerous place in the world for Jews as it fails to promote a secular Jewish identity. Furthermore, the suffering caused by Israel to Palestinians has not been accidental but is an inevitable outcome of Jewish ethnocentrism. In a moving tribute to Mordechai Vanunu, Heilbronn stated that Vanunu moved beyond his ethnocentric perspective and into an anthropocentric identity and consciousness when he blew the whistle on the secrets of the Dimona nuclear facility in Israel. He paid for his bravery when the Mossad apprehended him in Rome. He was charged with treason and aggravated espionage in a secret trial held in 1986. His prison term is for 18 years. According to Heilbronn, Mordechai's new anthropocentric identity results in Vanunu belonging to the human race and not a more limited individual identity. He has transcended loyalty to an ethnic group to a loyalty to humankind. The bridges between Vanunu's cause and other groups became clear when Heilbronn showed a recent photograph of Palestinian women holding pictures of both Vanunu and an imprisoned relative. Both men were being held in Ashkelon prison. Heilbronn urged the release of all political prisoners.

Three concurrent workshops were held in the morning. Participants in workshop one discussed Mental Health and Human Rights. Professor James Graff of the University of Toronto presented his paper on Mental Health and Respect for Human Rights. Professor Graff declared that basic elements involved in supporting human rights involve community support and the maintenance of strong family relations. The Rev. Dr. John R. Van Eenwyk of the University of Washington Medical School delivered a presentation on The Psychology of Oppression. Dr. Van Eenwyk stated that oppression causes people to seek safety behind the brutality those members of their own group employ in the name of keeping them safe. This creates a trauma cycle wherein people seek to protect themselves by traumatizing others, who then respond in the same manner. Only when oppressed people of all groups realize they have more in common with each other than with members of their own groups can the cycle be broken. Dr. Mohamed Mandour of the Palestine Red Crescent Society spoke on Mental Peace of Palestinians and their Lack of Fundamental Human Rights. He stated that Palestinians had nothing to do with the suffering caused to the Jewish people before the creation of Israel and so it is unfair for them to pay the price now. There is an urgent need to support and protect Palestinian human rights in order to alleviate the suffering imposed by Israel both before and after the peace process.

The second morning workshop dealt with Violations of Women's Rights. Ms. Zeinab El-Ghoneimi of the Ministry of Labor in the PA discussed The Consequences of Ignoring Palestinian Working Women's Rights. Ms. El-Ghoneimi stated that current law does not effectively protect working women, but there is an effort in the Palestinian Legislative Council to protect working women and their rights by providing maternal leave, sick leave, and time each day for breast-feeding. Dr. Muhammad M. Haj-Yahia of The Hebrew University delivered a presentation on Wife Abuse and Battering in Palestinian society: The Incidence of the Problem, Some of its Mental Health and Marital Consequences, and Women's Strategies of Coping. He stated that it is difficult in some instances to even gather information on the situation faced by women in Palestine because the husbands of some women prevent them from replying to questionnaires. His research study indicated that 40-50 percent of Palestinian women are punished either physically or psychologically by their husbands. Ms. Tonia Govender of The National Institute for Crime Prevention and Rehabilitation of Offenders - Durham Office spoke on Abused Women: The Way Forward. She stated that under the former South African government, women were raped by members of the police forces. Women were also tortured. Police forces are currently reluctant to involve themselves in cases of domestic abuse.

The morning's third workshop was on Rehabilitating Survivors of Human Rights Violations. Dr. Binnie Kristal-Andersson, a clinical psychologist and psychotherapist from Sweden, presented a paper on Human Rights and Psychodynamic Psychotherapy Supervision/Training of The Gaza Community Mental Health Programme's Staff members - A Focus on the Former Political Prisoners. She posited that many of the GCMHP staff had been imprisoned and abused by the Israelis. She then raised the question of how such traumatic experiences affected their work. She then stated that the GCMHP staff's experience as political prisoners deepened their understanding of commitment, taught them teamwork and cohesion skills, and gave them an acute awareness of their own capabilities and limits. Mr. Ahmad Abu Tawahina of the GCMHP then delivered a paper entitled The Social Network of Support as a Focus of Trauma and Treatment in Torture Victims: The Palestinian Experience, in which he stressed the importance of networks of support to rehabilitation efforts. Religion, the family, and professional and community networks are central to a torture victim's rehabilitation. It was such networks which the Israelis targeted and sought to destroy. Dr. Satia Advan of The Human Rights Foundation of Turkey - Istanbul Office then followed with a description of Turkish torture practices in her paper, An Evaluation of 182 Torture Survivors. As in Palestine, Advan argued that a mechanism of systematic torture aimed at political opposition existed in Turkey. Professor Federico Allodi and Dr. Sameh Hassan, two Canadian psychiatrists, rounded off the workshop with a discussion of how Western models of psychotherapy could be applied to Arab patients. In their paper, entitled Western Psychotherapy with Arab and Islamic Patients: A Cross-Cultural Model, Hassan and Allodi argued that, first, Arab patients are receptive to Western methods and theory, and that, second, one need only adapt such methods to the native environment. They described the therapist as a stage director in charge of organizing the patient's narrative into a history compatible with Western therapeutic methods.

Six workshops were held in the afternoon on a variety of subjects. The first workshop covered Mental Health and Human Rights. Dr. Moshe Landsman of Israeli Mental Health Workers for the Advancement of Peace (IMUT) delivered a paper entitled, Rights from Within: The Phenomenology of Health and Empowerment. He detailed the prison experience of ex-prisoner Emad Saba' through the use of Amedeo Giorgi's four basic criteria of analysis of human experience: meaning attribution, sociality, historicity, and expressiveness. Ms. Fiona McKay of Redress delivered a paper on Reparation for Torture in National Legal Systems. She insisted that there should be financial compensation to those people exposed to torture. Dr. Nuray Karali of The Human Rights Foundation of Turkey - Istanbul Office spoke on Forced Migration, Torture, and Related Psychological Problems. Dr. Karali cited her research study on immigrants, stating that many suffer from Post Traumatic Stress Disorder, anxiety, depression, and loss of hope. Professor Aliki Coudroglou of Arizona State University spoke on Human Rights, Mental Health, and Human Welfare. Coudroglou stated that culture plays an important role in affecting human rights and human welfare.

The second afternoon workshop was on The Violation of Children's Rights. Ms. Anja Meulenbelt, a mental health trainer, gave a talk entitled Domestic Violence in the Context of Political Turmoil. Her first point was that as the level of political violence rises, so does the level of domestic violence. Aggression, she said, which during times of political turmoil was channeled outward toward a common enemy, is now turned inward. Dr. Jomanah Odeh of the Palestinian Happy Child Center delivered a speech on Children with Special Needs: Their Rights and Future. She sounded what became a main theme in the workshop, that "the Palestinian child cannot attain the international standards of health until the Palestinian people are liberated from continuing occupation." The point was made, however, that this fact in no way excuses the Palestinian people of their own responsibility toward their children. Dr. Fadel Abu Hein of the Gaza College of Education delivered a presentation on Palestinian Children under Political Suffocation. He discussed how a new social class is emerging: working children. The closures imposed by the Israelis have had a devastating effect on the Palestinian working class. Children have consequently been enlisted to work long hours under dangerous conditions. Despite the difficulty of effecting change under continued occupation, the solution is partly the responsibility of the Palestinian people.

The third afternoon conference was on Public Education Strategies. Ms. Sibongile Ngobese of the Rape Crisis Community Outreach Project delivered a presentation on Developing Partnerships with the Police: A Strategy for Public Education in South Africa. She stated that there has been a recent, small improvement in police respect for the human rights of the people in South Africa. There is now a better relationship between the police and the public. Furthermore, NGOs are working to improve police awareness of the importance of human rights. Ms. Beryl Cheal of Portland State University spoke on Helping Young Children Cope with the Trauma of Violence: Strategies for Schools. She stated that therapy is vital for children who have endured traumatic experiences and that schools have a crucial role to play in helping develop healthy children. Ms. Maureen Kushner of The Kids' Comedy Club spoke on Peace Through Humor: Laughter is the Shortest Distance Between Two People. She stated that children can be helped a great deal by getting them to laugh, dance, and enjoy themselves through comedy. A comedy club can help provide children with knowledge of the world's problems, but in an environment in which they can still enjoy themselves.

The fourth afternoon workshop was on Preventing Torture. Professor Richard Garfield of Columbia University delivered a speech on The Impact of Economic Embargoes on Human Rights. He stated that mortality, infectious disease, and general health have all deteriorated in Iraq due to the deleterious effects of the embargo. Dr. Umit Bicer of Kocaeli University spoke on Human Rights Education. He asserted that physicians participate in torture by practicing medical exams on people without their consent, by not reporting instances of torture, by providing medical knowledge to torturers, by providing medical assistance without denouncing torture and simultaneously tendering their resignations, and by preparing false health certificates and/or autopsy reports. Professor Joop de Jong of the Free University of Amsterdam and TPO - Amsterdam delivered a presentation on The Program of the Transcultural Psychosocial organization (TPO) on the Psychosocial Consequences of Man-made Disaster in Eight African and Asian Countries. He stressed the importance of developing sound coping strategies for those traumatized by war. Administrators, social workers, and psychologists should work together in order to help the traumatized to cope with their experiences. Ms. Anne-Laurence Lacroix, in a talk entitled, Preventing Torture, stated that human rights NGOs need to redouble their efforts in pressuring governments to cease the practice of torture. International pressure, particularly in the form of the UN, should continue to be utilized to urge an end to torture.

Mr. Michael Adams of the Palestinian Studies Trust began the fifth workshop, The Media and Human Rights, with a talk entitled, A Free Society Needs a Free Press. As the government has various weapons it can use against the media - and has, as the PA has amply demonstrated - the truth is the principle weapon of the media, and it must be used if the body politic is to remain healthy. Mr. Moshin El-Franji, a Palestinian journalist, echoed the words of Adams. His talk, The Palestinian Media: Enhancing or Violating Human Rights, posed exactly that question. He answered by asserting that the PA has greatly hampered the Palestinian media's freedom of expression. "We are forced to turn to the Israeli media for information," he said. The only thing the Palestinian media enhances is PA policy. Mr. Ala' El-Mash'harawi of Al-Quds and Solidarity International for Human Rights, then took up the themes El-Franji brought up. His talk was entitled, Media: The Missing Link in Palestinian Citizen's Rights. People are the source of power, he said, and public opinion needs to be unfettered, not predisposed. However, the seemingly single voice of the panel was challenged during discussion time. Some Palestinian conference participants reminded the journalists that they have a responsibility to serve the social structure. "We reject fragmentation," as one participant said. Another participant pointed out how severely the continuing occupation affects the media.

The sixth workshop, The Violation of Children's Rights, began with a talk by Dr. Ahmad Abdul Baqi of the Uppsala University Hospital in Sweden. His speech, Childhood Trauma as a Vulnerability Factor, discussed how trauma is induced by incidents of violence or mistreatment. Though physical trauma tends to heal relatively quickly, psychological trauma may leave a lasting imprint on the child. Ms. P.C. Yogaranjanakumari, of Survivors Associated, Sri Lanka, gave a talk entitled Children of War: Stress Management for Caregivers. She supported Dr. Abdul Baqi's assertion that psychological trauma often disrupted the individual's social network. Dr. Brian K. Barber of Brigham Young University, USA, analyzed the effects of the Intifada on Palestinian children in his talk, Palestinian Child and Adolescent Experience During and After the Intifada. He found that children who participated in the Intifada, among other affects, withdrew socially and were less bound by the authority of their parents. Positive family relations also tended to protect children from the negative effects of the Intifada. Ms. Ayda Kassab and Dr. Samir Qouta, both of GCMHP, then delivered the talk, Trauma Dynamics in Children. The main point of the talk was that children passive during the Intifada suffered more than children who played an active role in the uprising.
