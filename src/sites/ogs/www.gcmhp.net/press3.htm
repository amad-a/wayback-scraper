PRESS RELEASE
GAZA COMMUNITY MENTAL HEALTH PROGRAMME
THIRD INTERNATIONAL CONFERENCE
OCTOBER 13-15,1997
HEALTH AND HUMAN RIGHTS
DAY 3 - OCTOBER 15

The Gaza Community Mental Health Programme‘s third international conference held its third day of activities today in the Rashad El-Shawa Cultural Center in Gaza City, Palestine. The theme of this year’s October 13-15 conference is Health and Human Rights. The conference brings together over 400 health professionals, scholars, and social activists from throughout the world to discuss the still-grim situation faced by Palestinians in the occupied territories and Palestinian semi-autonomous territory.

The morning Plenary Session brought together a distinguished panel of health professionals, scholars, and social activists. The first speaker was Psychiatrist and veteran African National Congress member Dr. Freddy Reddy who delivered a presentation on Suppression of Human Rights: Psychopathology. Dr. Reddy asserted that over the years doctors have contributed to tremendous wrongs perpetrated against innocent people. Sterilization and dubious theories about minority groups are marks on the record of the health profession. Since the 1970s, however, doctors have become more involved in promoting human rights.

Dr. Reddy linked the experiences of families in South Africa to those in the Gaza Strip when he spoke of suspicion and mistrust being rampant within families in both places. The reality was everybody was a potential “sell-out.” In an implicit difference with Gaza he mentioned how the South African Truth Commission has investigated the manner in which clandestine apartheid organizations worked to stop, undermine, suppress, and torture opponents.

Dr. Reddy spoke of how in the Gaza Strip almost all the people suffered from violations of their human rights. Palestinian children were the main victims and Post Traumatic Stress Disorder continues in them long after the actual experience of trauma. The treatment of personal suffering resulting from trauma involves a long process, but doctors and health personnel have an obligation to help. Doctors have heard the stories and must take responsibility for rendering assistance.

Another ongoing problem for Gazans is unemployment as it destroys self-respect and leads to despondency. Unemployment and the resulting consequences can happen at any moment with the closing of the border. He asserted, however, that people will only take so much before they rise up against the suppression of their rights.

Dr. Juliet Van Eenwyk of the Washington State Department of Health spoke on Epidemiology and Human Rights. In her presentation she outlined ways in which epidemiology can help protect human rights. She asserted that epidemiology can be used to improve health in instances in which people are deprived of their human rights. Epidemiology can bring human rights abuses to light and determine the effectiveness of efforts to raise levels of health and human rights. She outlined a number of examples in which epidemiology can help improve basic health conditions. In one instance, epidemiologists pinpointed the location of the worst outbreak of Japanese encephalitis in a Cambodian refugee camp and in another case were able to raise questions about whether girls under five in a Kurdish refugee camp were getting adequate and clean water compared to boys. She also outlined how epidemiologists were able to show impaired mental development in children exposed to high levels of chemical pollution compared to a similar cohort exposed to a good deal less chemical pollution.

Dr. Khadija Safwat of the Middle Eastern and African Research Centre - Wales spoke on States and People under Siege: The Political Economy of Collective Siege and Human Rights. She declared that all states are under some form of siege because of the reproduction of capital, the lack of loyalty to the state, and the cross-boundary nature of the flow of capital. She spoke of looking at the situation today as a series of sieges with a siege of Israel, a siege of the Palestinian Authority, and indeed a self-imposed Palestinian opposition siege.

Dr. Safwat stated that Israel is an expensive ghetto that costs the West and the U.S. a great deal. Outside Jewish contributions to Israel may start to decline as Jews begin to feel increasingly uncomfortable with giving so much for building civil society there. Israel is faced with the prospect of losing funds in the near future and developing a sense of coming under siege. As a result, it may be less willing to inflict a siege on the Palestinian economy although it will have to compete economically with the Palestinian entity more and more.

The final speaker of the Plenary Session was Dr. Abdel Aziz El-Rantisi of the Islamic University. He spoke on Islam and Human Rights. Dr. El-Rantisi stated that ethics are to protect human dignity and this is what Islam does. El-Rantisi spoke of racial discrimination in the U.S. and the violation of human rights by Israel against its Sephardic citizens. He also mentioned that Arab municipalities in Israel get smaller budgets than their Jewish counterparts.

Dr. El-Rantisi asserted that Jews could only find refuge from European discrimination in this region. Consequently, he declared that Islam is the best means by which to protect human rights. Under Islam, man can vote and attain public posts based on qualifications and not corruption. Unfortunately, many of those elected in the elections or appointed to posts in the Cabinet of the PA are not qualified.

Dr. El-Rantisi proceeded to discuss the protections offered by Islam to members of minority religions. Killing, according to the Quran, is prohibited except in certain prescribed situations and there is a heavy penalty for the violation of this prohibition. Furthermore, there are specific sayings not to kill oneself.

El-Rantisi declared that the best struggle is in speaking truth in front of an unjust leader. During the question and answer session El-Rantisi stated that the Islam he had spoken of was the one sent by God and agreed that there are currently no good examples of Islamic governments for Palestinians to follow.

An Awards Ceremony was held after the Plenary Session to honor people and organizations making a strong contribution to health and human rights in the Gaza Strip. Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj called on Israel and the Palestinian Authority to release all political prisoners and announced that letters to Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu and President Yasser Arafat regarding health and human rights concerns were being circulated for the signatures of conference participants. Dr. Haidar Abdel Shafi was the first to receive an award and was given a warm and moving tribute by Dr. El-Sarraj for his many years of work against the Israeli occupation and for the deep sense of integrity he exhibited in stepping down from the Palestinian Legislative Council (PLC) in a show of displeasure over the lack of respect accorded the legislative branch by the executive branch of government.

Other recipients of awards were Ruchama Marton, Inge Genefke, Barbara Harrell-Bond, David Henley, Khader Shkirat, Rami Heilbronn (whose denunciation yesterday of the Israeli authorities for preventing several conference guests from entering the Gaza Strip was covered in Al-Hayyat, while his denunciation of the ongoing imprisonment of political prisoner Fat’hi Suboh was ignored), the Medical Foundation for the Care of Victims of Torture, and the Union of Palestinian Medical Relief Committees. Awards were later presented to Lea Tzemel and the Public Committee Against Torture in Israel (accepted by Ms. Hannah Friedman.)

 Three concurrent workshops were held in the morning. The first workshop discussed the subject of Children. Ms. Frances Moore of Save the Children, UK, discussed the origin and objectives of her organization. Her talk, entitled The Rights of the Vulnerable Sectors in Society - Children’s Rights and the Work of SCF asserted that “The key factor affecting the Palestinian children in the occupied territories wathe the closure.” Ms. Moore co-presented with human rights specialist, Dan Seymour. Mr. Seymour identified the UN Commission on Human Rights as the best forum for addressing violations of children’s rights.

Dr. Tom Gumpel of Hebrew University argued that Israeli law systemically discriminates against children with special needs, both Jewish and Arab. His talk, Special Education Law and Policy in Israel: De Facto Segregation!, ended with the assertion that the rights of the disabled are a barometer for determining how truly segregated Israeli society is. There is certainly a relationship, he said, between the Israeli policy to discriminate against children with special needs, and its policy of separation between Arabs and Jews.

Dr. Abdel Aziz M. Thabit of the GCMHP investigated the effects of the occupation and the Intifada on the emotional health of Palestinian children. He delivered a disheartening presentation, citing how scarred Palestinian children were by the Israeli occupation – and how this scar is being transmitted transgenerationally. His talk was entitled, Post Traumatic Stress Disorders in Children in War. In response to a question about how Palestinian children could begin the process of normalization, Thabit made clear that until the children are allowed access to basic needs, and conditions of poverty and overcrowding are alleviated, normalization cannot take place.

 Participants in the second morning workshop discussed Public Education Strategies. Abdel Hamid Afana of the GCMHP discussed Human Rights and Mental Health: Community Mental Health Training in Palestine. He asserted that it is important for physicians to understand the possible mental health problems of patients who merely describe physical ailments.

Dr. Augustin Velloso of the Spanish Open University discussed Palestinian Politics and Education on the Threshold of the New Era. Dr. Velloso maintained that education should include play and other activities that bring joy to children.

Dr. Blair G. Van Dyke of Brigham Young University presented a presentation entitled, Education in an Autonomous Palestine: Palestinian Perspectives. Dr. Van Dyke argued that education serves as a basis for everything. One positive of the peace agreement is that Palestinians are now better able to pursue their education. He added that a Palestinian curriculum should be developed based on Palestinian standards.

 The topic of the third morning workshop was Women’s Rights. Mr. Ibrahim Khashan of the Palestinian Center Against Violence discussed Women’s Rights in Tribal Traditional Law - The Gaza Strip. Khashan pointed out that women who are raped have no rights in tribal traditional law and that women who bear sons are looked upon as better than those who bear only daughters.

Mr. Monzir Emad and Ms. Iman Redwan of the Women’s Affairs Center presented a paper on Early Marriage in Palestinian Society. They described discrimination against Palestinian women. Such discrimination prevents women from choosing their husbands. This decision is usually made instead by the woman’s father, brother, or a male relative. They also outlined the consequences of early marriage on women’s health. These consequences include psychological problems and marital difficulties.

Dr. Sylvie Mansour of Birzeit University presented a paper entitled, The Current Situation of Adolescent School Girls in the Ramallah Region. She outlined some of the problems faced by young women in Ramallah. These problems include family troubles, difficulty in traveling abroad and building friendships outside of the family, and domestic violence.

 Three workshops were held in the afternoon. The first workshop covered the subject of Rehabilitating Survivors of Human Rights Violations. Dr. Samir Qouta of the GCMHP spoke on Prison Experiences and Coping Styles Among Palestinian Men. Dr. Qouta outlined his research findings that Palestinian men developed seven distinct styles of coping with their prison experiences.

Dr. Ramadan T. Kodaih of the Gaza College of Education spoke on The Effects of Torture on Aspects of Personality: An Analytical, Descriptive Study of Palestinian Ex-Detainees. Dr. Kodaih presented his study on the connection between torture and personality. He stated that Israeli interrogation methods aimed to destroy the Palestinian detainee and not just extract information.

Mr. Jawad Abu Harb of the Palestinian Youth Union delivered a presentation on The Role of Nurses in Rehabilitating Torture Survivors. Mr. Abu Harb asserted that it is important to involve nurses in the rehabilitation of torture survivors.

The second afternoon workshop dealt with the issue of Mental Health and Human Rights. Dr. Mahmoud Sehweil of the Mandela Institute delivered a paper entitled, Rehabilitating Victims of Human Rights Abuses. Dr. Sehweil detailed several cases of Palestinian families in which family members suffered from trauma and depression as a result of Israeli violence directed against them.

Dr. David Henley of University Children’s Hospital, Sweden, Dr. Henrik Pelling of University Hospital, Sweden, and Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj of GCMHP spoke on Provision of Mental Health Services Through Network Participation. They stressed the importance of determining where mental health services are needed and of providing these services for children and adolescents. Such services should also help parents better deal with their children.

 Dr. Fat’hi El-Woheidi of Al-Azhar University began the third and final afternoon workshop on the subject of Ethics and Torture with a talk entitled Torture and the Role of Medical Associations. El-Woheidi argued that legal protections must be instituted to protect doctors who dare to speak out against torture practices. His talk ended with a call to justice; he urged that torture not be tolerated in any of its forms.

Mr. Abdel Rahman Abu El-Nasir of the Bar Association - Gaza Strip followed. In his talk, The Role of the Bar Association in Human Rights, he outlined the two roles of the Bar Association. First, the Bar Association has a responsibility to publicize information, in particular, information on Palestinian prisoners and academic detainees. Secondly, the Bar Association has the duty of advocacy. It must campaign against injustice and advocate the rights of Palestinian prisoners.

In the next talk, Palestinian Prisoners’ Conditions in Israeli Jails, Mr. Faris Abu Hassan of Solidarity International for Human Rights - West Bank, asserted that Palestinian political prisoners – including administrative detainees – in Israeli jails were provided with substandard legal and medical services. Doctors, he asserted, have even assisted in the torture of prisoners.

In the closing ceremony, Dr. Eyad El-Sarraj sounded a call for the release of Fat’hi Suboh. In strident words, he invited the PA to base its strength on a respect for law and human rights; “only then,” he said, “would we gladly sacrifice all we had for the Authority”.

Riyad El-Za’noun, the Palestinian Minister of Health, then charged the conference participants with bearing the burden of a message, that the Palestinian people demand justice. As in dark times, when God sent his emissaries to deliver messages of peace, Za’noun called the participants “prophets”, whose mission was to return to their homelands and resound the call of justice and the cry of injustice on behalf of the continuing Palestinian struggle. Za’noun then denounced what he termed Israel’s “starvation policy”. He angrily declared that the withholding of funds due Palestine by Israel and the international community claimed several thousands of victims, of which the nascent Palestinian infrastructure was one. “I have forgotten I am a minister,” Za’noun said, referring to the fact that the ministries have been closed for over a month.

Finally, he delivered a moving tribute to his mother, an 89-year-old woman who, as Za’noun said, was believed to be crazy by her grandchildren because, though she wept for the Palestinians massacred at El-Aqsa and Hebron, she wept too for the Israeli victims of the latest suicide bombings. “The Israelishave mothers too,” she said. Za’noun related that when his mother asked him who the conference attendees were, he told her, “They are people like you, who weep when Palestinians die, who weep when Israelis die, and who rejoice when peace arrives”. “Tell them,” his mother replied, “Um Salim is with them”.
