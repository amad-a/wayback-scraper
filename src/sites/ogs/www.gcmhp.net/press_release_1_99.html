The Public Committee Against Torture in Israel
Press Release

Upcoming Israeli Supreme Court hearing,Wednesday Jan. 13th 1999 in front of a nine-judge panel regarding 3 principal petition of the Public Committee Against Torture, 1 petition of the Association for Civil Rights in Israel, 1 Petition of Attorney Andre Rosenthal and 1 petition of Hamoked - Center for the Defense of the Individual on the legality of the use "moderate physical pressure" during interrogation of Palestinian detainees by the Israel General Security Services.

Following the invitation of the Public Committee Against Torture in Israel (PCATI),
Amnesty International observer form London, Joanna Oyediran, will attend the court hearing,

Yesterday PCATI, submitted to the Israeli Supreme Court a sworn statement of Robert Nigh Jr., the attorney for Timothy James McVeigh, accused of causing the Oklahoma city bombing in 1995 in which 168 people were killed.  It was the largest terrorist attack ever committed upon American soil.  In his affidavit, Attorney Nigh described, how despite the imminent threat of further bombings, US interrogators did not use any physical force and allowed Attorney Nigh to be present during the entire interrogation.

In addition, PCATI submitted a report by the Psychiatrist Dr. Avi Raps regarding the long-term devastating effects suffered by 14 Palestinians, aged between 26-28, who were interrogated by the GSS during and after the Intifada.

The committee also submitted to the court, research regarding different interrogation methods of detainees, suspected in terrorist activities, in Britain and the USA where torture is never used.

Over a thousand Palestinians are interrogated and tortured annually by the Israeli General Security Services (“GSS”) through severe physical and psychological methods. GSS interrogation methods include forced squatting, tying up detainees in contorted positions for long periods, handcuffing them to pipes, violently shaking their heads and bodies, shackling them for many hours in tight handcuffs and squeezing their arms and legs, forcing them to eat off bathroom floors, covering their heads with a wet and/or stinking sack, solitary confinement, exposure to extreme cold and heat, sleep denial for days, exposing them to blaring loud music 24 hours a day.  The UN Committee Against Torture declared in 1998 that these methods constitute torture and called on Israel to refrain form using these methods.

Over the past several years, PCATI filed several principal Petitions to the Israeli High court of Justice challenging the legality of these interrogation methods. On Jan. 13th, the Israeli High Court will hold a hearing in front of nine-judge panel to hear oral arguments by PCATI and the State on these petitions.  The case is expected to be seminal; its outcome will determine whether torture is legal or illegal in Israel.
