Epidemiology of stress related psychiatric disorders among the adults in Gaza Strip



This study is part of a multi-site transcultural research program for the identification, management and prevention of psychosocial and mental health problems of refugees and victims of organized violence in collaboration with the TPO (Transcultural Psychological Organization, Holland).
The overall goal is to provide a profile of mental health in Gaza Strip, in an attempt to find starting points for the development and application of community based intervention programs. The specific goals of the study are:
  1. To assess the extent and nature of exposure to trauma in the Palestinians of Gaza.
  2. To identify psychological consequences of trauma, and the prevalence of stress related psychiatric disorders.
  3. To identify different risk factors associated with psychiatric disorders.
sample: A multi-stage random sampling procedure was used to get a representative sample of the heterogeneous population in Gaza. Refugees are not restricted to the camps, as some of them have moved to the cities and others were displaced to new resettlement areas. Likewise, the resettlement areas already contained people from the cities who moved to live in new areas. Eight field researchers with university degrees were trained for two weeks on the instruments. Data collection was achieved in less than five months.
The sample included adult males and females aged 16 to 60 from the different cities, camps and resettlement areas of Gaza Strip, excluding subjects with severe cognitive impairment who can not respond to questionnaires. Through this 600 respondents were interviewed in their homes, and the questionnaires were completed.

Instruments:

The instruments included tools to assess the extent of exposure to trauma and stress in different life periods, in addition to the standard demographic data on age, gender, employment, housing conditions etc.

1. Demographic data: this form covers general information about the interview (location, date), the interviewer (name etc.) and respondent (age sex, marital status etc.). at the end of the form there is a list of some physical health complaints.

2. The Trauma and life events questionnaire: TPO has developed a trauma and life events questionnaire based on the experience of several clinicians from several ethno-cultural backgrounds. This instrument was used to cover the range of traumatic events experience by refugees before during and after their flight from their country of origin.
 
a) Child maltreatment: The early childhood traumatic experiences in the family context.
a. Conflict related trauma before 12 years of age.
a. Conflict related trauma after 12 years of age: the same checklist was used to assess the exposure to traumatic events after 12 years of age.
b. Daily hassles: Describe everyday nuisances that can affect people living in camp situations.
3. Symptoms Checklist-Revised SCL-90-R (Derogatis, 1977)
1. Composite International Diagnostic Interview 2.1(WHO, 1993)
2. Coping Questionnaire.
3. Social Support and Social Support provision questionnares.
4. Quality of life questionnaire (WHOQOL).
5. Disability Questionnaire.

Data analysis:

Data analysis was done in GCMHP with the assistance obtained from TPO through Mr. Ivan Komproe, Ph.D. , a statistician and psychologist. Mr. Komproe visited Gaza for two weeks and assisted the researchers in statistics and data analysis. His, and our researchers efforts on the SPSS resulted in generation of a huge database on the mental health status of Palestinians in Gaza. The process of finer analysis and write-up of the results is expected to continue the coming two or through years.

Results:

The initial results of the study are summarized here, although further analysis is still ongoing on the large amount of data available.

Two thirds of the respondents have either witnessed or suffered traumatic events (Annex 1 below). The most frequent traumatic event reported in the sample was witnessing death of a family member (42.2%), especially after 12 years of age. More than one third of the respondents have witnessed violent clashes with the army during their lifetime (35.1%), and this was more frequent after 12 years of age (32.9%).

Parenting related trauma exposure: 36.6% reported at least one type of child maltreatment during their childhood, most commonly verbal abuse by parents and elder family members. The prevalence of severe child maltreatment was reported by 6.4% of the respondents.

Property loss due to the occupation or Intifadha was reported by 11.5%, most commonly reported was loss of a house (4.1%) or personal property (3.4%)

The life time prevalence of stress related psychiatric disorders was found to be 34.2%. Males showed more disorders than females, and refugees compared to citizens.

PTSD was the most frequent stress related diagnosis found in the sample( n= 114, 19.5%). It was more frequent in males than females. This difference between males and females in the lifetime prevalence of PTSD disappears when controlling for the exposure to trauma.
PTSD was found to be the only diagnosis in 46 out of 102 cases (45%), which constitutes 7.8% of the whole sample. PTSD was associated with other psychiatric disorders in 55% of the cases (30.4% with major depression, and 26.5% with anxiety, 19.6% with somatoform disorders).

Interesting correlation was found with early child maltreatment and the exposure to daily hassles. The section on peritraumatic dissociative responses also showed very strong correlation with the occurrence of PTSD later.

Major depression was found as a life-time diagnosis in 17.6% of the sample. Although males had a higprevalence than females, the difference was not statistically significant(19.7%, 15.8%). Refugees had a higher rate compared to citizens (20.5%, 13.6%). Among the important risk factors associated with depression, daily hassles experienced by people living in the camps were positively correlated with depression, followed by trauma experienced below 12 years of age. Unemployment in the 40 to 60 age group was highly correlated with depression.

Other psychiatric diagnoses investigated included anxiety disorders and somatoform disorders. They were found as life time diagnosis in 13.2 and 5.3 percent respectively.

The second step in data analysis will be the investigation of patterns of psycological and social responses to trauma and stress in the sample. The sections on peritraumatic dissociative phenomena, coping and social support show significant patterns of correlation with stress related disorders and psychological symptoms tapped by the SCL-90-R. It is still early to present the results of these sections since they are still under investigation.
